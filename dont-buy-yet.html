Don't buy this yet:
seven purchases worth pausing on.

We make money when you buy things. So take this page in the spirit it's written: these are the purchases where waiting, or buying something cheaper, is the better call. Nothing here is a claim that a product is defective — each entry is a specific reason to pause, drawn from our own reviews.

The DJI Mini 5 Pro "Fly More Combo Plus"Buy the cheaper kit

The Plus battery is rated at 52 minutes, which is genuinely tempting. But it unambiguously pushes the aircraft past 250 g — which means FAA registration, a number marked on the airframe, and Remote ID, exactly the paperwork the Mini line exists to avoid. The Plus packs aren't sold in the EU at all.

If the sub-250 g class is why you're buying a Mini, buy the standard kit and skip the Plus battery.

Any dash cam with a monthly subscriptionSkip entirely

A number of dash cams now gate cloud storage, parking alerts or even basic footage access behind a monthly fee. Our position is simple: avoid any dash cam that charges monthly for features that should be built in. Our top pick does front-and-rear Starvis 2 at $149 with no subscription, which makes the recurring-fee models hard to justify.

A solar charger chosen by its wattage numberDon't buy for that reason

Rated watts are measured at panel-face irradiance you will not see in the field. In testing, the Goal Zero Nomad 20 delivered 8 W of its rated 20 W, and the Anker Solix PS30 delivered 14 W of 30 W. The rating isn't a lie, it's a laboratory ceiling — but buying on the headline number means buying roughly half the panel you think you are.

A Bluetooth speaker bought for its battery specDon't buy for that reason

Speaker battery ratings are measured at a volume nobody actually uses. The JBL Charge 6 is rated 24 hours and measured around 13; the Bose SoundLink Flex is rated 12 and lands nearer 7–10. Buy these for sound and build — they're good — but halve the battery number before you plan a weekend around it.

A $79 "beginner" droneSkip entirely

Most beginner drone advice is wrong. A toy-grade drone doesn't teach you to fly — it teaches you that flying is frustrating, then breaks. The right first drone for the overwhelming majority of people is a real one, and our beginner guide names it. Spend once.

A drone for real-estate work, before Part 107Sort the paperwork first

If you're flying to photograph listings, that's commercial work and Part 107 certification is not optional — the FAA has fined agents who didn't know that. The certificate is the prerequisite, not the camera. Buy the drone second.

An e-reader, before you pick the bookstoreDecide the ecosystem first

The hardware is the cheap part of this decision. The bookstore you commit to costs several times more than the device over five years, and switching later means abandoning a library. Choose Kindle or Kobo on the terms of the store, then buy whichever reader that store implies.

None of the above is an allegation that a product is faulty. Every entry is either a spec that independent testing didn't reproduce, a business model we think you shouldn't reward, or a piece of sequencing advice — and each links to the review with the evidence in it.
