⚡ Nerd Box: How We Verify a "Deal"

Retail "was" prices are the most gamed number in commerce — anchor a fake $499, "discount" to the everyday $299, and the brain's loss-aversion circuitry does the rest. So we don't take was-prices at face value. Before a deal makes this page, we open the Amazon listing, read the price off the live page, and check who is actually in the buy box.

There are no "Lowest Tracked Price" badges on this page today, and that is deliberate. That badge is a claim about price history, not about today's price, and this run we could not re-open our price-history source to re-confirm the two badges the page carried. An unverifiable badge is worse than no badge, so both came off. They come back when we can check them, not before.

Who is selling matters as much as the price. Four deals here — all three drones and the Osmo action cam — have a marketplace seller in the buy box rather than Amazon or DJI, even though Amazon ships them. Marketplace buy boxes rotate: the price and the seller can both change without the listing changing. So those four link to an Amazon search rather than a single listing, and each card names the seller we saw. The other nine are sold and shipped by Amazon and link directly.

No badge means it's a genuine discount, but the product has hit this price (or close) before — several deals here sold for less during June's Prime event or the July 4th weekend, and we say so in each card rather than pretend otherwise. When a deal is mediocre we say that too (see the Osmo Action 5 Pro), and when one gets worse we say that as well (see the DJI Neo, up $30, and the OM System kit). Amazon's own promotional badges — "30% off," "365-day low price" — are their arithmetic against their list price, not ours; where our anchor is more conservative than theirs, we publish the smaller number.

The Tricks We Refuse To Use

Deal pages are persuasion machines, so here's ours with the hood open. What you'll find here: real deadlines, real price history, real savings math. What you will never find here: countdown timers (they're theater — the clock resets), "only 3 left!" stock claims (we don't have Amazon's inventory data, and neither do most sites that show them), inflated "was" prices (the oldest trick in retail), or fake editor's awards. If a page needs to lie to sell you something, the deal was never good. Ours don't need to.
