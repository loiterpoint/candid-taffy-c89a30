How We Evaluate Power Banks

Loiter Point does not run a test lab, and we won't pretend otherwise. Power banks are a category where that honesty matters more than most, because the industry's favorite spec — rated mAh — tells you surprisingly little about what reaches your phone. Our rankings synthesize published lab measurements, primarily TechRadar's tested power bank rankings (real efficiency percentages, measured charge counts, and power-to-weight math on every unit), cross-checked against long-term owner reports that surface what short review cycles miss: swelling cells, dying displays, and charge counts that fade after six months. Where a manufacturer's rated figure and measured reality disagree, we print both and label them. Prices were checked in July 2026 — this category discounts constantly, so treat list prices as ceilings.

Nerd Box: Why a 10,000mAh Bank Charges a 5,000mAh Phone 1.8 Times, Not 2

Power bank cells store energy at 3.7V, but your phone charges at 5V or 9V, and voltage conversion burns energy as heat. The industry rates capacity at the cell voltage — the marketing number — so a "10,000mAh" bank realistically delivers 60–70% of that to your device. That's why TechRadar's measured "number of charges" figures (1.8 for the 10K banks here, 3.5 for the 20K ones) are the most honest spec in this guide, and why measured efficiency separates good banks from bad ones: the Anker 737's 89% at 100W is exceptional, and cheap no-name banks can dip below 55%. One more number worth knowing: airlines cap carry-on batteries at 100Wh, which works out to roughly 27,000mAh — that's why serious travel banks like the 737 stop at 24,000mAh, and why anything bigger belongs at home.

What We'd Skip

Three honest warnings. First, skip any bank sold purely on a giant mAh number with no wattage or efficiency data — a 30,000mAh bank that converts at 55% delivers less usable energy than a good 24,000mAh one, slower. Second, skip magnetic banks that aren't Qi2 certified; the older 7.5W standard turns "convenient" into "overnight." Third, if you fly, skip anything over 27,000mAh entirely — airlines enforce the 100Wh limit at the gate, and a confiscated power bank is the worst value in tech.
