How We Evaluate

We don't run a cutting lab. Loiter Point ranks tools by reading the outlets that do — Pro Tool Reviews, Bob Vila, Tool Box Buzz and others who put saws through timed rip and cross-cut runs — and cross-checking their findings against owner reports at the major retailers. Where a number comes from the manufacturer's spec sheet we mark it (rated). Where it comes from a reviewer's own bench we mark it (reported). We don't publish first-hand cut times because we don't generate them.

Circular saws split into three body styles: the common sidewinder (motor beside the blade, blade usually on the right), the rear-handle/worm-drive layout (motor behind the blade, blade on the left, more grunt), and the compact 6-1/2 in. class (lighter, shorter cut depth). We picked one standout per role rather than five near-identical 7-1/4 in. sidewinders, because the right saw depends more on how you hold it and what platform you already own than on a single "best" score.

On price: street prices below are the tool-only or corded figures we saw in mid-July 2026 and they move constantly — bare tools go on sale, kits with a battery and charger cost meaningfully more, and battery platforms carry their own hidden cost (see the lock-in note lower down). Treat every dollar figure as a checkpoint, not a quote.

The Picks

01 — Best Overall Cordless
Milwaukee M18 FUEL 2732-20TOP PICK
~$219

Pro Tool Reviews has kept the 2732 at or near the top of its cordless circular saw rankings for years, and the reason is sustained cutting rather than a single fast cut. In their testing the saw made a full-depth cross-cut on a 6x12 header in about four seconds and ripped ten feet off a 2x12 in roughly 35 seconds (reported) — numbers Milwaukee frames as matching a corded 15-amp saw. The redesigned brushless motor spins to 5,800 RPM (rated), and the electric brake stops the blade fast when you release the trigger.

The catch is the platform. This is a bare tool; the performance reviewers describe assumes Milwaukee's High Output batteries, and a full HD12.0 pack plus charger is a separate, non-trivial purchase. If you're already on M18, that's a sunk cost and this is close to a default recommendation. If you own nothing, factor the battery into the real price before comparing it to the corded pick.

02 — Best Rear-Handle
Makita XSR01 18V X2 (36V)BLADE-LEFT
~$229

The XSR01 isn't a true worm drive — Pro Tool Reviews and Tool Box Buzz both note it uses a helical gear set that meets at 45° rather than the 90° of a real worm — but it puts the blade on the left and the handle behind the motor, which is the sightline and balance worm-drive users expect. Reviewers reported it running around 5,100 RPM (rated), roughly 1,000 RPM faster than a typical corded worm drive, and Home Fixated's verdict was that it out-cuts a corded worm drive in practice. It runs on two 18V LXT batteries in series for 36V.

It is not light. With two 5.0Ah packs it lands near 12.4 lb (reported), which is heavier than most sidewinders and part of why the blade-left, rear-handle crowd likes it — the mass sits where a worm-drive user wants it. The trade is the same platform math as the Milwaukee: you're feeding it two batteries, not one, so runtime and cost both scale with pairs.

03 — Best Corded Classic
Skilsaw SPT77WML-01 Worm DriveCORDED
~$159

The SPT77WML is the worm drive that dropped the weight. Reviewers at Tool Nerds, Tools In Action and others consistently call it the lightest saw in its class — around 11.6 lb (reported) thanks to a magnesium body and shoe, against the 15-plus lb of traditional cast worm drives. A 15-amp dual-field motor turns it to 5,300 RPM (rated), it cuts 2-3/8 in. deep at 90° (rated), and it bevels to 53° with positive stops at 0° and 45°.

Because it's corded there's no battery math and no runtime ceiling — plug it in and it cuts until you stop. Reviewers flag the trade-offs: the base is hard plastic with minimal rubber bumpers, and the cord is thin for jobsite abuse. It's a worm drive, so blade-left sightlines and worm-drive maintenance habits apply. For anyone who doesn't want to buy into a battery platform at all, this is the value-per-cut leader here.

05 — Best Compact 6-1/2 in.
DeWalt DCS565 20V MAX (6-1/2 in.)COMPACT
~$119

Not every cut needs a 7-1/4 in. saw. The DCS565 is DeWalt's brushless 6-1/2 in. compact: a shorter blade, a lighter body, and a saw you can steer one-handed through plywood and trim. It's rated to 4,950 RPM with a 2-1/8 in. depth of cut at 90° (rated) and bevels to 50°. DeWalt puts the blade on the left, adds a rafter hook and LED, and includes an electric brake — the same feature set as the full-size saws, in a smaller package.

The shorter blade is the whole point and the whole limit: 2-1/8 in. of depth handles sheet goods, decking and most trim, but it won't through-cut a 4x4 in one pass the way the 7-1/4 in. saws will. On the DeWalt 20V MAX platform it shares batteries with a large tool line, so for owners of that system it's a cheap, light second saw rather than a compromise primary.

What to Know Before You Buy

// Nerd Box — Sidewinder vs. Worm-Drive / Rear-Handle

A sidewinder puts the motor beside the blade and drives it directly, usually with the blade on the right. It's lighter, spins faster, and most people learn on one. A worm drive (or a rear-handle saw that mimics it) puts the motor behind the blade and gears the power down through a right-angle set — lower RPM, more torque, and the blade on the left.

Two things follow. First, torque: the geared layout holds speed better in thick or wet stock, which is why framers and concrete-form crews favor it. Second, sightline: with the blade on the left, a right-handed user looks straight down the cut line instead of over the motor — better visibility on long rips, but the opposite of what a lifelong sidewinder user's muscle memory expects. Neither is "better." Pick the layout you can see and balance, then argue about torque.

// Nerd Box — Battery Platform Lock-In Economics

With cordless saws, the sticker price is the smaller number. Four of five picks here are bare tools, and each is captive to one battery system — M18, LXT, ONE+, 20V MAX. The 36V Makita wants batteries in pairs. A high-output pack and charger can cost as much as the saw, so the real question isn't "which saw is cheapest" but "which platform do I already own, or want to be married to for the next decade."

If you own nothing, the corded Skilsaw sidesteps the entire calculation: no packs, no charger, no runtime ceiling, no platform. If you already have a drill and impact on one color, buying the matching bare-tool saw is usually cheaper per capability than switching. Count the batteries before you count the saws.

Bottom Line

If you're on Milwaukee M18 or buying into one pro platform, the 2732 is the saw reviewers keep ranking first. Worm-drive users who've gone cordless should look at the Makita XSR01 for the blade-left layout. If you don't want a battery fleet, the corded Skilsaw SPT77WML gives you worm-drive feel at the lowest cost-per-cut. The Ryobi PBLCS300 is the value entry for ONE+ owners and DIYers, and the DeWalt DCS565 is the light compact for 20V MAX owners who don't need full 2x depth. There's no single winner — there's the layout you can see down and the platform you already own.
