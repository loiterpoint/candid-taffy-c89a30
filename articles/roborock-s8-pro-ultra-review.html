Roborock S8 Pro Ultra Review (2026): Still Worth It Now That It's on Discount?

By the Loiter Point desk · Updated July 2026 · ~9 min read

The Short Version

  • What it is: The Roborock S8 Pro Ultra was the brand's 2023 flagship — a self-emptying, self-washing, self-refilling vacuum-and-mop combo with the RockDock Ultra base. It has since been superseded, and in mid-2026 that means a street price around $900, down from a $1,599 launch MSRP.
  • Where it shines: Independent testers report excellent hard-floor and low-pile pickup (RTINGS measured 97.9% of debris removed by weight), genuinely useful robotic mopping, and dual rubber rollers that owners say devour pet hair without tangling.
  • Where it stumbles: It leans on structured-light navigation, so it struggles in full darkness; the rated 6,000 Pa suction only shows up on the loudest modes; and high-pile/shag carpet is a weak spot.
  • Verdict: At ~$900 it's a strong value on a mature, well-understood platform. At its old $1,599 price, skip it — and if you want the newest navigation, look at the Saros 10R instead.

How We Evaluate

Loiter Point does not run a robotics lab, and we won't pretend otherwise. What we do is synthesize published independent measurements — RTINGS-style debris-removal and airflow tests, sound-meter readings from multiple outlets, and battery run-down logs — alongside verified owner reports about the things a two-week loaner never surfaces: mop-pad durability, dock reliability, and how a machine ages. Every specific number below is traceable to that published work, and where the evidence conflicts we say so rather than inventing a clean answer. Rated figures come from Roborock; anything labeled "reported" or "est. real-world" comes from that outside testing.

The Dock Is the Real Product

The robot is competent; the RockDock Ultra is why people buy this thing. It empties the onboard bin into a sealed bag, washes the mop pads, dries them with warm air to fight the musty-mop smell, and refills the robot's clean-water tank between runs. In practice that means you interact with the S8 Pro Ultra roughly once a month — to swap the dust bag and top up the clean-water reservoir — rather than daily. Independent reviewers consistently rate this as one of the more reliable all-in-one docks of its generation, which matters, because a flaky dock turns a hands-off robot back into a chore.

The trade-off is footprint and noise. The dock is bulky and needs a permanent home near an outlet, and the auto-empty cycle is loud (more on that below). It also drinks water: if you mop a large area daily, you'll refill the clean tank more often than the "once a month" ideal suggests.

Vacuuming: Great on Hard Floors, Honest Limits on Carpet

On bare floors and low-pile rugs, the S8 Pro Ultra is excellent. In RTINGS' testing it removed 97.9% of test debris by weight, and the DuoRoller dual rubber brush is the standout: owner reports repeatedly praise how it pulls pet hair without wrapping into the tangled bird's-nest that plagues bristle brushes. If you have a shedding dog or long human hair, that single design choice is worth a lot.

The suction story needs an asterisk. Roborock rates the machine at 6,000 Pa (HyperForce), but independent testers note airflow runs below that headline figure in everyday modes — you only approach the rated number on "Max" or "Max+," which drain the battery fast. Some reviewers also found crevice pickup underwhelming relative to earlier Roborocks. High-pile and shag carpet is the clear weak spot: reviewers report the robot struggling, occasionally beaching itself, and leaving debris behind on deep pile. On tight-weave and low-pile carpet, though, it does a genuinely good job.

Nerd box · the noise ladder

Robot-vacuum "loudness" is meaningless without the mode. Independent sound-meter readings put the S8 Pro Ultra roughly here: about 61 dB in normal vacuuming (a lively conversation), ~64.5 dB reported in quiet mode, up to ~69 dB on carpet boost, and ~76.7 dB at Max+ — approaching a traditional upright vacuum. The real jolt is the dock: auto-empty spikes to about 82.2 dB for ~25 seconds, comparable to a leaf blower. It's brief, but schedule cleanings for when you're out if that bothers you or a pet.

Mopping and Navigation

The VibraRise 2.0 system uses a sonically vibrating pad that scrubs rather than just dragging a wet cloth, and it lifts the pad on carpet so you're not smearing dirty water across rugs. For daily maintenance mopping — dust, light footprints, the kitchen floor — reviewers rate it well. It is not a substitute for a hands-and-knees scrub on dried, stuck-on messes, and some owner reports flag mop-function quirks and durability complaints over longer ownership, which is worth weighing against the value case.

Navigation uses structured light and infrared sensors rather than a camera. Upside: fast, tidy mapping and no camera pointed around your home. Downside that testers consistently call out: in complete darkness it struggles to see obstacles, so a hallway smart light or leaving a lamp on during night runs helps. Battery is a 5,200 mAh pack; reviewers report up to roughly 180 minutes in quiet mode, but expect closer to half that on Turbo — one outlet logged a 63-minute vacuum-and-mop run taking the battery from ~80% to ~10%. It self-recharges and resumes, so whole-home coverage isn't a problem; the number just isn't the "3 hours" you'd plan a marathon around.

Bottom Line

The Roborock S8 Pro Ultra is a case study in why waiting pays off. As a $1,599 flagship it was very good but hard to justify. As a ~$900 machine in 2026, it's one of the better value plays in premium robot vacuums: a proven, low-maintenance dock, excellent hard-floor pickup, capable daily mopping, and a pet-hair brush that owners genuinely rave about. Go in clear-eyed about the limits — the 6,000 Pa rating is a best-case number, shag carpet isn't its friend, and it wants a little ambient light at night. If those aren't dealbreakers for your home, it's an easy recommendation at the discounted price. If they are, spend up for the S8 Max Ultra or the newer Saros 10R rather than paying near-original money for this one.
