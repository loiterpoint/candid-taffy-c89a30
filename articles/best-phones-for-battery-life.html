How We Evaluate Phone Battery Life

Loiter Point does not run a hardware lab. We do not own dozens of phones, we do not have a light meter pointed at a display rig, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. What we do is read the published measurements from outlets that do run controlled battery tests — Tom's Guide, GSMArena, PhoneArena, Android Central, Digital Trends — cross-reference them against each other, and weigh them against what owners report after weeks of ordinary use. When a number in this guide comes from somewhere, we name the source in the same sentence.

That approach has one unavoidable complication, and it is the single most important thing to understand before you read a battery ranking anywhere on the internet: runtime numbers from different publications are not directly comparable. A battery test is a recipe, and every outlet writes its own. The variables that move the result most are:

  • Screen brightness. Most standardized tests nail the panel to a fixed nit value. Different targets across outlets can swing a result by hours on a large OLED.
  • Network. A test run over cellular — with the modem negotiating, hunting for signal and drawing power — drains a phone considerably faster than the same workload run over Wi-Fi. This is the big one, and it is why the Moto G Power's number below is not what it looks like at first glance.
  • Workload. Endlessly reloading web pages is not the same as gaming, navigating, video calling, or shooting 4K. Mixed-use drain tests routinely land well below web-surfing tests on the same handset.
  • Software state. Firmware version, background sync, adaptive refresh behaviour and thermal conditions all shift results, which is why the same phone can post different figures months apart.

So we rank by the strength and consistency of the evidence, not by lining up numbers from five different labs and sorting the column. Where a figure appears in a specs grid below, it is labelled "est. real-world" or "reported" and attributed. Nothing here is labelled "measured" by us, because we did not measure it.

Prices are manufacturer MSRP at the time of writing. Street prices on phones move constantly — carrier promos, trade-in credits and seasonal discounts routinely knock triple digits off flagships — so check the listing before you commit. We do not quote live pricing.

Tom's Guide recorded 25 hours 13 minutes for the OnePlus 15 in its battery test and reported it as the longest result the publication has logged. That is the headline, and it is a genuinely unusual one — battery leaderboards normally move in fifteen-minute increments, not by opening a gap this wide over the field.

The mechanism is not mysterious. OnePlus fitted a 7,300 mAh cell, a figure that would have meant a brick-thick chassis a couple of generations ago, and paired it with a current-generation Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5. Capacity plus an efficient SoC plus OxygenOS 16's power management is a boring, effective combination. Practically, this is the phone to buy if your complaint is that you are hunting for outlets by mid-afternoon. Read this before you read the number. The Moto G Power (2026) is reported to run over 27 hours in a Wi-Fi browsing test. That is a bigger figure than the OnePlus 15's 25 hours 13 minutes, and it does not mean the Moto lasts longer. The two results come from different protocols. A Wi-Fi browsing loop is generally a gentler workload than a cellular web-surfing test, because the modem is not doing the expensive work of maintaining and negotiating a mobile connection. Putting these two numbers side by side and declaring a winner would be straightforwardly misleading, so we are not doing it, and you should distrust anyone who does.

What we can say with confidence is that a 6,720 mAh cell in a $299.99 phone with a modest chipset is a well-established recipe for excellent real-world endurance. Motorola's G Power line has built its identity on exactly this, and owner reports have consistently supported it across generations. This is a phone that survives a long day and then some, without the flagship price.

The rest of the package is more sensible than exciting: IP68/IP69 ingress protection and Gorilla Glass 7i are unusually robust at this price, Android 16 is current, and the 50MP rear camera is adequate rather than notable. Nerd box: why mAh stopped predicting runtime

Two things are happening at once in 2026, and they pull in different directions.

The first is chemistry. Silicon-carbon anode batteries are the reason the OnePlus phones fit 7,000-plus mAh cells into a chassis you can still put in a pocket. Replacing part of the conventional graphite anode with silicon raises energy density, so a given physical volume holds meaningfully more charge. That is a real, hardware-level jump rather than a spec-sheet trick, and it is why the capacity figures at the top of this list look implausible next to phones from a few years ago.

The second is that capacity has never been the only variable, and it is becoming a worse predictor every year. The Galaxy S26 Ultra versus Pixel 10 Pro XL comparison above is the clean example: the Pixel has the larger 5,200 mAh cell and reportedly runs roughly two hours shorter, posting 9 hours 53 minutes against the Samsung's 16 hours 10 minutes in Tom's Guide's test. The difference is efficiency — SoC process node, display panel characteristics, and how aggressively the software manages background work and refresh rate. A more efficient chip on a newer node does the same work for less energy, and a better panel spends less power hitting the same brightness.

The practical takeaway: treat mAh as one input among several, and weight published runtime results from a consistent protocol far more heavily. One more piece of context, offered honestly — some phones sold only outside the US, including the Oppo Find X9 Pro and Xiaomi 17 Ultra, post even longer runtimes in overseas testing. We are not linking them because they are not practical US purchases: band support, warranty and software update paths all get complicated. But if you see them topping global battery lists, that is why.

Bottom Line

If battery life is the thing you are actually shopping for, the answer in 2026 is the OnePlus 15 at $899 MSRP. The evidence is unusually clear: Tom's Guide recorded 25 hours 13 minutes and reported it as the longest result the publication has logged, and no other widely-available US phone is close in that same test. If $899 is more than you want to spend, the OnePlus 15R at $699 sits second on the same list and is the better-value buy.

Under $300, the Moto G Power (2026) is the obvious budget choice, as long as you understand that its 27-hour figure comes from a gentler Wi-Fi test and is not evidence that it beats the OnePlus. If you are staying on iOS, the iPhone 17 Pro Max is the endurance pick at $1,199 — with the honest caveat that independent results for it range from roughly 11.5 hours to nearly 18 depending on workload. And if you want the S Pen and the 200MP camera, the Galaxy S26 Ultra at $1,299 gives you the best battery result in Samsung's lineup alongside everything else.

The meta-point is worth repeating, because it will serve you long after these five phones are discontinued: a runtime figure is only meaningful attached to its protocol. Brightness, network and workload move results by hours. When you see two numbers from two publications compared directly, that comparison is decorative. Check what was actually run, and weigh capacity as one factor rather than the answer.
