How We Evaluate Sleep Trackers

Loiter Point does not run a sleep lab. We don't strap on ten wearables and claim to have "tested" them against a hospital polysomnograph — almost nobody outside a clinical research group actually does that, and the ones who do publish their work. So our job is to read that published work carefully, cross-check it against verified owner reports, and translate it into a buying decision.

The anchor for staging accuracy in this guide is a Brigham and Women's Hospital study (published in Sensors, 2024) that compared several consumer wearables head-to-head against polysomnography (PSG) — the clinical gold standard, where a technician scores your sleep stages from brain waves, eye movement, and muscle activity. That study is the single best apples-to-apples comparison of these devices' four-stage classification, and it does most of the heavy lifting below. We also lean on a 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (OTO Open) that placed Oura against medical-grade sleep studies, plus manufacturers' own published validation figures — clearly labeled as such, because a company grading its own homework is weaker evidence than an independent lab doing it.

Everything in the spec grids that reflects real usage — battery life you'll actually see, staging performance — is labeled "reported" or "est. real-world." Manufacturer ratings are labeled as ratings. Where the independent evidence is thin (Ultrahuman's validation record, for example) or the pricing is all over the place (Withings), we say so plainly rather than papering over it. That's the whole point of the site.

// Nerd Box: reading the accuracy numbers

Why "79% agreement" and "kappa 0.65" are the numbers that matter

When a tracker brags about accuracy, ask accuracy at what. There are two very different tasks hiding under the word "sleep."

Total sleep time is the easy one: did you sleep roughly seven hours, and when did you drift off and wake up? Almost every tracker in this guide nails this. Movement and heart-rate patterns make the sleep/wake boundary fairly obvious, so duration figures tend to be trustworthy across the board.

Four-stage classification — correctly labeling each chunk of the night as Wake, Light, Deep, or REM — is the hard one. A clinician does this from brain waves in a polysomnography (PSG) sleep study; a consumer device is inferring it from your pulse, temperature, and motion. That's why staging is where devices separate.

Two numbers cut through the noise. "Agreement with PSG" (e.g., Oura's ~79%) is the share of the night the device labeled the same stage the lab did. But raw agreement flatters a device, because some stages dominate the night — guess "Light" a lot and you'll be right often by luck. That's what Cohen's kappa corrects for: it measures agreement beyond what chance would produce, on a scale where 0 is coin-flip and 1.0 is perfect. In the Brigham and Women's study, Oura's kappa of 0.65 (moderate-to-substantial) beat Apple Watch's 0.60 and Fitbit's 0.55. Those gaps are small in absolute terms but meaningful — and they're the honest way to compare staging, precisely because kappa refuses to reward lucky guessing. So when you see a great total-sleep-time claim, remember: everyone hits that. The kappa is where the truth lives.
