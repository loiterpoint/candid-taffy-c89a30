How We Evaluate Soundbars

Loiter Point does not run a test lab, and we won't pretend otherwise. Soundbars are a category where that honesty matters, because "Dolby Atmos," channel counts, and quoted wattage are the three things marketed most aggressively and understood least. Our rankings synthesize published independent measurements — primarily from RTINGS, whose soundbar bench is the most thorough in the business, plus What Hi-Fi? and Consumer Reports — cross-checked against long-term owner reports that surface what a one-week review misses: HDMI handshake bugs after a firmware update, subwoofers that drop their wireless link, and rear speakers that need their own power outlets. The most useful thing independent labs do here is measure a bar's actual frequency response, maximum clean loudness, and how convincingly it steers height and surround effects — not just repeat the box's channel numbers. Where a spec and the measured reality diverge, we say so. Prices were checked in July 2026; soundbars discount harder and more often than almost any other tech category, so treat list prices as ceilings and the street prices below as the number that actually matters.

Nerd Box: What "11.1.4" Actually Means — and Why "Atmos" Isn't One Thing

The three numbers on a soundbar box decode cleanly. The first is ear-level channels (fronts, center, surrounds); the second is subwoofers; the third is height channels that create overhead sound. So the Samsung's 11.1.4 means eleven ear-level speakers, one sub, and four upward-firing height drivers, while the Sonos Beam's effective 5.0 means no separate sub and no dedicated height drivers at all. That third number is where marketing gets slippery: a bar can claim "Dolby Atmos" two very different ways. Real Atmos uses up-firing drivers that bounce sound off your ceiling so effects come from above — the Samsung, Sony and Hisense do this. Virtual Atmos, like the Beam's, uses processing to fake height from front-firing drivers; it can widen the sound but can't put a helicopter over your head. Neither is fraud, but they are not equivalent, and the box rarely tells you which you're getting. One more spec that decides real-world happiness: HDMI eARC vs. ARC. Only eARC has the bandwidth to carry full, uncompressed Atmos and DTS:X from your TV to the bar; plain ARC compresses it. Every pick here supports eARC — but if you pair any of them with an older TV that only has ARC, you quietly lose the very format you paid for.

What We'd Skip

Three honest warnings. First, don't buy a flagship bar-only system thinking you're done — the Sonos Arc Ultra and Sony Bar 9 are superb, but without their optional subs and rears they can't match a complete 5.1.4 or 11.1.4 kit for surround, and those add-ons cost real money. If you want full surround on one invoice, the Samsung and Hisense are the honest answers. Second, be skeptical of cheap bars advertising huge "Dolby Atmos" in the title with only two front-firing drivers; that's virtual height at best, and often just a stereo bar with a processing badge. Third, match the bar to your TV's HDMI: you need an eARC port to get uncompressed Atmos, and if you game at 4K/120 you want either a bar with its own HDMI 2.1 input (the Sony) or a spare 2.1 port on the TV. Buy for the room and the sources you actually have, not the biggest channel number on the box.
