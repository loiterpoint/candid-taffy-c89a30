How We Evaluate Gaming Monitors

Loiter Point is an aggregation shop, not a test lab, and we think that's worth saying loudly on a page like this one. Display testing done properly is expensive and slow — you need a calibrated spectrophotometer, a high-speed camera for pursuit photography, an input-lag tester with a known-good reference, and a controlled dark room. Plenty of sites that write about their own bench results own none of that. Rather than pretend, we read the outlets that publish full methodologies and raw measurement tables, weigh their results against one another, and layer in owner reports from retailer reviews and enthusiast forums for the failure modes bench tests don't catch: panel lottery, coil whine, firmware bugs, how the warranty claim goes when you file one.

That means labeling matters more here than anywhere else on the site. A number carrying rated came off a spec sheet and represents a best case under conditions the manufacturer chose — response-time figures in particular are almost always the fastest single grey-to-grey transition on the panel, not an average. A number carrying mfr. claim is a marketing statement nobody independent has verified. A number carrying measured by RTINGS came from a published test with a stated procedure and is the only kind of figure we'll let carry real argumentative weight. Where sources conflict — and on street pricing for one of these picks, they do — we say so instead of quietly picking the flattering number.

For monitors specifically, we weight in roughly this order. Panel technology and motion clarity come first, because they set the ceiling on everything else and are the hardest thing to fix with settings. Then the resolution-to-refresh balance measured against what a GPU can realistically push — a spec you can't feed is a spec you didn't buy. Then input lag, because it's cheap to get right and inexcusable to get wrong. Then HDR capability, judged on whether the panel has the contrast hardware to do anything with an HDR signal rather than on badge tier alone. Then connectivity and ergonomics, which are what you'll resent daily if they're bad. Price-to-performance breaks ties, and at the bottom of the range it does more than break ties — it decides the pick outright. If your needs are broader than gaming, our general monitor guide weights things differently, and the home office roundup puts text clarity and USB-C docking ahead of refresh rate entirely.

Nerd Box: Your GPU sets the refresh rate, not the panel

A 240Hz monitor fed 90 frames per second is a 90Hz monitor with a bigger sticker. Variable refresh rate keeps that from tearing, but it cannot invent frames — the panel simply holds each one longer, and the motion clarity you paid for evaporates. Before you buy refresh rate, check what your card renders in the games you play at the resolution you want. 4K 240Hz is a serious ask; hitting anywhere near it in modern titles generally means upscaling and frame generation are doing meaningful work.

The second half of this is that the returns curve is brutally non-linear. Going from 60Hz to 144Hz is a change almost anyone notices immediately — frame time drops from about 16.7 ms to about 6.9 ms, and the difference in cursor tracking and camera panning is obvious without training. Going from 240Hz to 360Hz moves frame time from roughly 4.2 ms to 2.8 ms. That 1.4 ms is real and measurable, and competitive players at a high level can exploit it, but it is a fraction of the perceptual jump you got the first time. If you're choosing between 360Hz at 1440p and 240Hz at 4K, the framing is that you're choosing between a small motion-clarity edge and a large resolution one — and for most people, most of the time, the pixels win. The same arithmetic applies to gaming laptop panels, where the marketing runs even further ahead of what the mobile GPU can feed.

#5 Budget HDR

AOC Q27G3XMN

~$279 (drops to $249–260)
The only sub-$300 monitor where HDR means something

One clarification before anything else, because it trips people up: some Amazon listing text for this model erroneously states 3440×1440. The actual resolution is 2560×1440. It is a standard 16:9 27-inch panel, not an ultrawide, and if you're buying it expecting the latter you'll be disappointed for the wrong reason.

With that out of the way — this is a VA panel with a mini-LED backlight split into 336 local dimming zones, certified VESA DisplayHDR 1000, at typically $279 and sometimes as low as $249. That combination is essentially unheard of at this price, which is why it gets cited relentlessly as the standout budget HDR display. VA already starts with far better native contrast than IPS, and 336 zones of backlight control on top of it means highlights punch and dark scenes stay dark instead of grey. The gap to a $1,000 OLED on HDR impact is real but much smaller than the gap in price. Refresh is 180Hz over DisplayPort 1.4 and 144Hz over HDMI 2.0 — worth knowing if you were planning to run it off a console, where you'll be capped at 144Hz. Two HDMI 2.0 and two DisplayPort 1.4 inputs, a height-adjustable stand, and a three-year zero-bright-dot warranty round it out. The rated 1 ms GtG is optimistic for VA in the usual way; VA's characteristic weakness is smearing on dark transitions, and no spec sheet has ever advertised that. Nerd Box: OLED burn-in in 2026, and what mini-LED still does better

Burn-in is no longer the reason to avoid OLED, and the clearest evidence for that is contractual rather than technical. Manufacturers now write burn-in coverage into the warranty explicitly — GIGABYTE's three years on the MO27U2 names it directly — and companies do not underwrite a failure mode they expect to see at scale. The mitigation stack has matured alongside it: pixel-shifting, per-pixel brightness limiters, automatic compensation cycles, heatsinks pulling thermal load off the emissive layer, and proximity sensors that blank the panel when you leave. Read the actual warranty terms before you buy, because coverage varies by brand and some policies exclude what they call "expected" uniformity change — but the era of treating burn-in as disqualifying is over.

What hasn't changed is the physics of the trade. OLED controls light per pixel, so a bright object against black has no halo at all. Mini-LED controls light per zone, and 336 zones spread across a 27-inch panel means each zone covers a substantial area — a starfield or white subtitles on a dark scene will show blooming that no algorithm fully hides. So OLED wins black levels outright. But mini-LED wins sustained full-screen brightness, and by a lot: OLED panels run automatic brightness limiters that pull back output when a large portion of the screen is lit, because driving every pixel hard at once is a thermal and longevity problem. A full white document, a snow map, a bright HDR sky — that's where an LCD backlight simply keeps going. If you game in a bright room, or leave the monitor on static bright content for hours, that limiter is a daily reality rather than a footnote.

Bottom Line

The interesting fact about this list in 2026 is how little you have to spend to get most of the way there. The ASUS ROG Swift OLED PG27UCDM is the best of these and RTINGS rates it the best it has tested, but the GIGABYTE MO27U2 delivers the same 4K 240Hz QD-OLED headline at $649.99 with burn-in written into the warranty, and unless you specifically need DP 2.1a UHBR20 or 90W USB-C docking, that is the pick that makes financial sense. If you play competitively and would rather have frames than pixels, the Alienware AW2725DF gets you 360Hz OLED for somewhere in the $500–600 range on sale — and we'll repeat that the reported sale figures conflict, so watch it rather than trusting any single number.

At the value end, the LG 27GR83Q-B at around $290 is the default recommendation for anyone who isn't sure — 240Hz, 3.2 ms of measured input lag, dual HDMI 2.1 for consoles, and no burn-in to think about. And if HDR is the thing you want and $300 is the ceiling, the AOC Q27G3XMN is the only answer, because 336-zone mini-LED at that price has no competition. Whichever you pick, check what your GPU renders first. A refresh rate you can't feed is money spent on a number.
