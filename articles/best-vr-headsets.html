How We Evaluate VR Headsets

Loiter Point does not run a test lab. We do not own a photometer, we have not strapped these headsets to a dummy head, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. What we do is read every credible independent measurement that has been published, cross-reference it against verified owner reports, and throw out anything that only exists in a manufacturer's press release.

That means the numbers in this guide fall into two buckets, and we label them. Rated figures are manufacturer specifications: panel resolution, refresh rate, advertised field of view, shipping weight. Those are useful for comparison but they are marketing numbers, and in VR they are unusually easy to game. Est. real-world and reported figures come from independent reviews and owner consensus — comfort over long sessions, how a headset actually behaves once you put a demanding title on it, what the practical total cost ends up being once you buy the accessories the box does not include.

Prices move constantly right now, and 2026 has been unusually ugly for that. A global surge in memory-chip costs pushed Meta to raise Quest 3 and Quest 3S prices on April 19, 2026 — the Quest 3 went up $100 and the Quest 3S up $50 — and Apple raised the Vision Pro from $3,499 to $3,699 in June for the same underlying reason. Prices in this guide are current as of July 2026. Where evidence is thin or reviewers disagree, we say so instead of splitting the difference and pretending it is a finding.

The Quest 3 wins on a boring but decisive argument: it is the only headset here that is genuinely complete out of the box. No PC, no console, no base stations, no separate controller purchase. You charge it, you draw a boundary, you are in VR in under ten minutes. Every other pick on this list has an asterisk attached to that sentence.

The pancake optics are the thing people underrate. Meta's earlier Fresnel lenses had a narrow sweet spot — text was crisp dead center and smeared toward the edges, so you learned to turn your head instead of moving your eyes. Pancake lenses largely erase that. Published reviews consistently describe the Quest 3 image as sharp edge to edge, and that changes what the headset is good for: reading, browsing, working in a virtual desktop, anything where you look around a scene rather than staring at its middle. Color-accurate passthrough is the other differentiator, and independent reviews still rate it the best mixed-reality passthrough available on a standalone headset.

Who should not buy this: anyone whose priority is contrast. The Quest 3 uses LCD panels, so black is dark grey, and space sims or horror titles will look washed out next to an OLED headset. If you already own a PS5 and mostly play seated cinematic games, the PSVR2 is the better screen for $200 less. And if you are price-sensitive and mainly play brightly lit action titles, the Quest 3S runs the exact same games on the exact same chip for $250 less.

If a PS5 is already sitting under your television, this is the most headset-for-the-money on the list. It launched at $549.99 in February 2023; Sony announced a permanent cut to $399.99 in February 2025, effective that March. That is not a sale — it is the price now.

The OLED panels are the reason to want it. LCD-based headsets like the Quests light their pixels from behind, so a black pixel is really a dimmed one. OLED pixels switch off entirely, which produces true blacks and an HDR range the Quests physically cannot match. In practice that means space, horror, and anything set at night looks dramatically better here — no grey haze where the void should be. It also has hardware the Quests do not: eye tracking, haptics built into the headset itself so impacts rumble against your face, and the adaptive-trigger controllers PS5 owners already know.

Who should not buy this: anyone without a PS5. It is not standalone and it is not a PC headset out of the box, so the real price for a non-owner is the headset plus a console. The other honest caveat is the library — it is curated and console-shaped rather than sprawling, and it does not approach the Quest catalog in raw volume. Buy it because you want a small number of excellent games to look genuinely spectacular, not because you want the most things to play.

Nerd box — optics, and why spec sheets lie

Pancake and micro-OLED are not just "better", they fix different problems

Two headsets can list identical panel resolutions and look nothing alike, because resolution is only one term in the equation. The lens decides how much of that resolution actually reaches your retina, and how evenly.

Fresnel lenses — the older design, still used on the Quest 3S — get their focusing power from a series of concentric ridges stamped into a thin piece of plastic. That makes them light and cheap, but those ridges scatter light. The consequence is a small sweet spot: the image is sharp where your eye is aligned with the lens axis and degrades as you look away from centre, plus you get visible ring artefacts wherever a bright object sits against a dark background. Pancake lenses fold the light path back on itself between polarised surfaces instead, which produces a much flatter, more uniform focus across the whole lens. That is why reviewers describe the Quest 3 as sharp edge to edge while the same reviewers describe Fresnel headsets in terms of a sweet spot you have to aim your head at. The panel did not necessarily change much. The delivery did.

Micro-OLED attacks a different axis. Because each pixel emits its own light and can switch fully off, contrast is effectively per-pixel — a black pixel next to a white one is genuinely black, not backlit grey. That is why an OLED or micro-OLED headset looks so much more convincing in dark scenes than an LCD one with the same nominal resolution. Perceived sharpness is partly a contrast phenomenon: edges read as crisper when the dark side of the edge is actually dark. This is the main reason a spec-for-spec comparison between an LCD and an OLED headset undersells the OLED.

Field-of-view numbers are not comparable across manufacturers

Treat every advertised FOV figure as brand-internal. There is no enforced industry standard for how it is measured, and the variables that move the number are all under the manufacturer's control: whether they quote horizontal, vertical, or diagonal; how far the lens sits from the eye when they measure; what interpupillary distance they set; whether they report the best case for one eye or the binocular overlap you actually perceive. Any of those choices can swing the published figure substantially, in the same way diagonal screen measurements make two differently shaped displays look equivalent.

The practical implication: a headset advertising a larger FOV than another is not reliably wider in use, and eye relief alone — how close the lens sits to your face, which changes with the facial interface, glasses, or a different gasket — can meaningfully change what you personally see. Compare FOV within a manufacturer's own lineup, where the methodology is at least consistent. Across brands, trust reviewers who look at multiple headsets under the same conditions over any number on a box.

Should you wait for Steam Frame?

Valve's Steam Frame is confirmed for summer 2026, with an expected price somewhere in the $899 to $1,199 range. It ships with a bundled 6GHz dongle for wireless PC streaming, which is the single most interesting confirmed detail — untethered PC VR without fighting your household Wi-Fi has been the missing piece for years.

The signals that it is genuinely imminent rather than vapour: the hardware has cleared US customs, the FCC filings are public, and Valve has a "Great on Frame" section live on the store. That is the shape a launch takes weeks out, not months.

What we will not do is tell you how it performs, because nobody outside Valve can source that yet. Our position is narrow and specific: if you are a PC VR buyer, it is reasonable to wait a few weeks and see. Even if the Frame disappoints, its arrival is likely to reset expectations and pricing in that segment. If you are buying standalone or for a PS5, the Frame is not aimed at you and there is nothing to wait for.

Bottom Line

For the overwhelming majority of buyers the answer is the Meta Quest 3, and it is not especially close. It needs nothing else to work, the library is the largest in VR, and the pancake optics deliver a sharp image across the whole lens rather than in a small central window. The April price increase to $599.99 stings, but it did not change what the headset is.

If $600 is more than you want to risk on a category you have not tried, the Quest 3S plays every single one of the same games on the same chip. Watch the price — near $300 it is one of the best deals in consumer tech, and at full list the case for spending up on the Quest 3's lenses gets stronger. If you already own a PS5, the PlayStation VR2 at $399.99 gives you OLED contrast, eye tracking, and headset haptics that no Quest can match, at the cost of a smaller catalogue.

The Bigscreen Beyond 2 is a specialist tool for people who already have SteamVR base stations and want to forget they are wearing a headset — brilliant for that person, an expensive trap for anyone else. And the Apple Vision Pro at $3,699 only makes sense if visionOS specifically is the thing you want, with the reported deprioritization of the line factored into your thinking.

One last piece of timing advice: PC VR buyers should sit tight for a few weeks. Steam Frame is confirmed for this summer, the hardware has cleared customs, and even an imperfect launch is likely to move prices in that corner of the market. Everyone else can buy today without regret.
