How We Evaluate Phones Under $1,000

Loiter Point does not run a lab. We do not own a light meter, a battery rundown rig, or a shelf of retail units. Anyone who tells you a five-person review site "tested 40 phones" is describing a spreadsheet, not a workbench. What we do instead is narrower and, we'd argue, more useful: we synthesise published independent measurements from outlets that genuinely do run standardised tests — Tom's Guide's battery rundown, PhoneArena's display and thermal work, the standard benchmark suites — and cross-reference them against verified owner reports gathered from retail reviews, long-term forum threads, and return-rate patterns.

That means three rules govern every number on this page. First, if a figure came from someone else's instrument, it is attributed to them by name. Second, anything we can only estimate from converging owner accounts is labelled "est. real-world" or "reported" — never "measured", because we did not measure it. Third, where the evidence is thin or the sources disagree, we say so in plain language rather than papering over it with confident prose. You will see that happen more than once below, including on the OnePlus 15's base price.

The ranking below is ordered by evidence and buyer fit. It is not ordered by price, and it is emphatically not ordered by what pays us most — the cheapest phone here is not last, and the phone with the highest sticker price is not first.

A word on the shape of this price band in 2026, because it explains a lot of what follows. The $799–$899 tier has quietly converged. Apple, Samsung and Google all now anchor their base flagships at a 6.3" display, and all three ship with roughly six to seven years of OS update support. That convergence is why the differentiators in this guide are not spec-sheet items like screen size or longevity — those are effectively table stakes now. The real gaps are in battery endurance, camera processing, silicon performance, and which software ecosystem you want to live inside for the next half-decade.

Nerd box: why 7,300 mAh fits in a normal phone now

For most of the last decade, phone battery capacity in this form factor stalled around 4,500–5,000 mAh. The constraint was volumetric energy density — graphite anodes take up a fixed amount of space per unit of charge, and phone chassis were not getting thicker. Silicon-carbon anode chemistry changes that arithmetic: silicon holds substantially more lithium per unit volume than graphite, and blending it into a carbon matrix manages the swelling that historically wrecked cycle life. The practical result is more capacity in the same physical envelope.

The OnePlus 15's 7,300 mAh cell is what that shift looks like at retail, and Tom's Guide's 25 hr 13 min average is what it looks like in an independent rundown test. That is not an incremental improvement over a typical flagship result — it's a category change, which is why their write-up framed it as the first phone they'd measured stateside to comfortably clear two days.

The related point worth internalising: fast charging and big capacity are no longer a trade-off. 80W SUPERVOOC pushing a reported ~45% in 15 minutes on a 7,300 mAh cell means the absolute energy delivered per minute is higher than on a smaller battery charging at the same rated wattage. Bigger battery, same charge time — that's the part that actually changes daily behaviour.

Nerd box: why Tensor benchmarks trail while Pixel photos lead

The Tensor G5 sits behind Snapdragon 8 Elite and MediaTek Dimensity 9400+ in published benchmarks, and Google has never seriously contested that. Standard benchmark suites weight CPU and GPU throughput heavily — sustained multi-core compute, rasterisation, memory bandwidth. Tensor allocates a disproportionate share of its die to the TPU/ISP block that runs Google's computational photography and on-device Gemini inference, and that block contributes almost nothing to a benchmark score.

So the two facts are both true and not in tension: the Pixel 10 Pro is slower on paper and produces better photographs out of the camera app. Which one matters depends entirely on what you do. Heavy gaming or sustained compute workloads will expose the gap. Shooting photos, running on-device AI, and normal daily use will not.

Bottom Line

If you want one answer and no further reading, buy the Galaxy S26 at around $799. It is the phone with no weak axis, and after Samsung's own cut from $899.99 it's priced level with the base iPhone while giving you the display and support story that owners rate highest.

Past that, the decision is genuinely about what you optimise for, and each alternative has a specific, evidenced reason to exist. Photographs and on-device AI point to the Pixel 10 Pro at around $899, with the honest caveat that its silicon trails the competition in published benchmarks. An existing iOS life points to the iPhone 17, which is a materially better buy than any base iPhone before it now that 256GB and a high-refresh display come standard at $799. Battery anxiety points to the OnePlus 15, and it points there with the only independently measured endurance figure on this page. And a tight budget points to the Pixel 10 — but only on a dip, and only if you can genuinely live with 128GB.

The thing we'd stress most: because Apple, Samsung and Google now all ship roughly six to seven years of OS updates on a 6.3" panel in this price band, whichever of these you buy, you are buying a phone you can keep. The differences that used to justify spending past $1,000 have mostly collapsed into this tier. The Galaxy Z Fold 8 and Z Flip 8 arriving on July 22 will be interesting phones, and they will also be well outside this budget — that's a separate guide, not a reason to wait on this one.
