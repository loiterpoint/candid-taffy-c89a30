The Best Mini PCs (2026)

Updated July 2026 · Consumer tech reviews built on real evidence

The Short Version

  • Best overall: the Apple Mac mini (M4, 16GB/256GB) — the most desktop-replacing machine in the category, as long as you can live with storage and memory that are welded shut forever.
  • Best Windows all-rounder: the Beelink SER8 — Ryzen 7 8845HS, 32GB, 1TB, and a vapor-chamber cooler that reviewers repeatedly single out for being quiet. Its Amazon price swings hard; check it before you buy.
  • Best value Windows box: the GEEKOM A8 Max — nearly the same Radeon 780M experience, dual LAN, USB4, and three-year coverage for meaningfully less.
  • Best budget / always-on box: the Beelink EQ14 — a quiet, low-wattage N150 box for Home Assistant, Docker, or a 4K media player. Not a gaming machine, and not really a work desktop.
  • Best for home lab & networking: the Minisforum MS-01 — dual 10G SFP+, dual 2.5G RJ45, a real PCIe x16 slot, and three M.2 bays in a 190mm cube.
Grab your pick: Mac mini M4 ($689.95) · Beelink SER8 ($939) · GEEKOM A8 Max ($719.10) · Beelink EQ14 ($329.99) · Minisforum MS-01 ($1,139)

The mini PC stopped being a compromise somewhere around the point that laptop chips got good. A modern 1-litre box running a Ryzen 7 8845HS or an Apple M4 will handle a browser with sixty tabs, a full IDE, Lightroom, a 4K timeline, and a couple of virtual machines without complaining — and it does it while drawing a fraction of the power of the tower it replaces, from a chassis you can hide behind a monitor.

The catch is that "mini PC" now covers everything from a $200 media box to a $1,500 workstation with 10-gigabit networking, and the listings do a spectacular job of blurring that line. The same CPU name appears in boxes that perform very differently. A headline price often turns out to be a barebone with no RAM, no SSD, and no operating system. And third-party seller pricing on Amazon moves so much that a "deal" today can be full retail next week.

Below are five machines worth buying in 2026, spanning roughly $330 to $1,600. Every performance claim here is either a manufacturer specification or a figure from a named independent reviewer or verified owner report — and where the published evidence is thin or contradicts itself, we say so rather than filling the gap with a guess.

How We Evaluate Mini PCs

Loiter Point does not operate a hardware testing lab, and we don't pretend otherwise. These rankings synthesize three sources: manufacturer specifications, published measurements and hands-on findings from independent outlets that do run controlled tests — in this category primarily ServeTheHome, Notebookcheck, TechRadar, Neowin, TweakTown, Creative Bloq, and StarryHope — and the pattern of verified owner reports from people running these boxes daily. Every real-world figure below is attributed to whoever produced it.

The things we weight most heavily are the ones that separate mini PCs from each other rather than the ones printed on the box. Sustained thermal behaviour and noise matter more than peak clock speed, because every machine here is a laptop chip in a small metal box and the cooler decides how much of that chip you get. Serviceability matters — whether RAM and storage can be replaced, and how many M.2 slots there are. I/O matters enormously, since a mini PC has no expansion bays and whatever ports it ships with are the ports it will have in five years. And we flag price volatility explicitly, because in this category the sticker is the least stable specification of all.

#1 · BEST OVERALL

Apple Mac mini (M4, 16GB / 256GB)

$689.95 on Amazon when checked · Apple's own store MSRP is $599 · check current price

The M4 Mac mini is the machine most people mean when they say they want a small computer that replaces a desktop. Ten CPU cores and ten GPU cores, 16GB of unified memory as the new baseline, near-silent operation, and a footprint that disappears under a monitor stand. For writing, development, photo work, and video editing, it is the least compromised small box you can buy, and at Apple's $599 MSRP it is arguably the best value in personal computing right now.

The problem is the configuration on this listing. A 256GB SSD fills up fast in 2026 — a couple of Xcode toolchains or one serious video project and you're managing free space weekly — and neither the storage nor the memory is user-upgradeable. Ever. Apple's upgrade pricing is steep enough that many owners simply hang an external portable SSD off the back and treat the internal drive as boot-only, which works well over Thunderbolt but is a workaround, not a fix. Note also the gap between the $689.95 Amazon price observed here and Apple's own $599: on this particular product, the direct-from-Apple route is often cheaper.

Port layout is thoughtful and slightly unusual: Thunderbolt, HDMI, and Gigabit Ethernet at the rear, with USB-C and a headphone jack moved to the front where you'll reach them. If you're driving more than one display or need legacy USB-A, budget for one of the USB-C hubs we've covered. Want more headroom without changing platforms? The M4 Pro model with 12 CPU cores, 16 GPU cores, 24GB and 512GB was $1,599.00 when checked — a large jump, but it fixes both of the base model's real weaknesses at once.

#2 · BEST WINDOWS ALL-ROUNDER

Beelink SER8 (Ryzen 7 8845HS, 32GB, 1TB)

$939.00 on the listing when checked · historically far lower · check current price before buying

The SER8 is the Windows box we'd point most people at. The Ryzen 7 8845HS is an eight-core, sixteen-thread part boosting to 5.1GHz with Radeon 780M graphics, and Beelink pairs it with 32GB of DDR5 and a 1TB NVMe drive — a configuration that doesn't need upgrading on day one, which is rarer than it should be. Both ServeTheHome and TechRadar reviewed it, and the consistent theme across their coverage is the cooling: a vapor chamber inside an aluminium chassis, which reviewers describe as unusually quiet for the performance on offer, alongside a maintenance-friendly internal layout. It has USB4 and can drive three 4K displays at once through HDMI 2.0, DisplayPort 1.4, and USB4 — useful if you're building out a multi-monitor home office.

Two honest warnings. First, price. The $939.00 we observed on this listing is well above what this machine has historically sold for — it has spent long stretches in the $500 to $600 range, and third-party seller pricing on Amazon swings hard enough that the number you see may bear no relation to the number above. At $939 it's a hard sell against the GEEKOM below; at $600 it's the obvious pick. Check before you commit. Second, the recurring criticisms in published reviews are consistent and worth taking seriously: price, wireless performance, and the absence of a VESA mount, which matters if your plan was to bolt it behind a display.

#3 · BEST VALUE WINDOWS BOX

GEEKOM A8 Max (Ryzen 7 8745HS, 16GB, 1TB)

$719.10 when checked · Ryzen 9 8945HS trim around $899 · check current price

The A8 Max is the pragmatist's answer to the SER8. The Ryzen 7 8745HS is a close relative of the 8845HS with the same Radeon 780M graphics, so day-to-day it feels much the same, and Notebookcheck framed the machine explicitly as a value play built on that chip. TechRadar's review highlighted the dual network ports and general connection flexibility, calling out creative studios as a natural fit — two independent NICs in a box this size is unusual, and it's what makes the A8 Max viable as a small server or a machine that lives on two networks at once. USB4, 8K output, and three-year coverage round it out, and the chassis is tiny at 135 x 132 x 45.9 mm and 685 grams.

Where it loses to the SER8 is memory: 16GB versus 32GB. That's fine for most work, but the Radeon 780M shares system RAM, so heavy multitasking plus graphics load will feel the pinch sooner — see the nerd box below on why single-channel and low-capacity RAM hurt an integrated GPU more than people expect. Verdict: at $719.10 against a $939 SER8, the A8 Max is the better buy. If the SER8 drops back toward its historical price, that flips.

#4 · BEST BUDGET / ALWAYS-ON BOX

Beelink EQ14 (Intel N150, 16GB, 1TB)

$329.99 for this 16GB/1TB trim when checked · check current price

The EQ14 is the machine to buy when the job is "be on all the time and use almost no electricity." Intel's Twin Lake N150 is a four-core, four-thread part turboing to 3.6GHz with Intel UHD graphics, and it's been reviewed widely — Notebookcheck, Neowin, TweakTown and StarryHope have all covered it. The consensus across those reviews is worth stating plainly: the N150 is a marginal step over the older N100. The turbo clock is higher and the graphics are nominally newer, but the real-world gain is modest. Don't pay a premium expecting a generational leap.

What reviewers do agree on is that the fundamentals are good: low noise, low power draw, two M.2 slots, and an internal power supply rather than a laptop-style brick. The limits are equally clear. The UHD graphics are entry-level and single-channel DDR4 holds the chip back further; published testing has Counter-Strike 2 at 1080p on lowest settings averaging under 20 fps, which is a polite way of saying this is not a gaming machine. The happiest owners run it as a Home Assistant or Docker host, a small Linux server, or a 4K media box — jobs where a 4C/4T chip sipping power is exactly right.

One pricing precision point, because it gets misquoted constantly: Notebookcheck titled its EQ14 review "Ready-to-go HTPC for under $200," and that sub-$200 figure refers to the cheaper 512GB trim, not this 16GB/1TB ASIN at $329.99. Both are real; they are not the same product. If 512GB is enough for your use case, the cheaper trim is the better-known bargain.

The MS-01 is a different species from everything above. It's roughly a 190mm square by 50mm tall, and Minisforum spent that volume on I/O rather than on being small: dual 10G SFP+ cages alongside dual 2.5G RJ45, two USB4 ports, HDMI, one PCIe 4.0 x16 slot (wired x8 electrically, sized for a low-profile card such as an RTX 3050), and three M.2 slots supporting 2280, 22110 and U.2 drives. TechRadar, Creative Bloq and a long list of home-lab writers have covered it, and the audience it landed with is obvious — this is a Proxmox node, a pfSense or OPNsense router, an all-flash NAS, or a small virtualization host, in a box that fits on a shelf.

That's a single owner report on one workload, not a controlled measurement, and your figure will move with drive count and what's loaded — but it's a useful sanity check that a 14-core box with 10G networking can idle sensibly.

The trap: there is a barebone i9-13900H version of the MS-01 (ASIN B0CT2FPD2C) with no RAM, no SSD and no operating system. The frequently-quoted ~$623 figure for the MS-01 refers to that barebone, not to the $1,139.00 complete 32GB/1TB machine linked here. Nerd box: the cooler decides how fast the chip is

Mobile AMD and Intel chips ship with a configurable TDP range, and the box builder picks where in that range to run. A Ryzen 7 8845HS configured at 54W and the same 8845HS configured at 35W are the same silicon with materially different sustained performance — they'll post similar numbers in a short burst, then diverge as soon as the workload runs long enough to heat things up. This is why two mini PCs with identical CPU names in the listing title can behave differently on a video export or a long compile, and it's why we weight cooling design over clock speed. It's also why reviewers keep mentioning the SER8's vapor chamber: a cooler with headroom lets the chip hold its boost instead of sawing up and down against a thermal ceiling. When you're comparing two boxes with the same processor, the useful questions are what TDP the vendor configured, what the cooling solution is, and what independent reviewers said about noise under sustained load — not the number of gigahertz on the spec sheet.

Nerd box: single-channel RAM quietly cripples an integrated GPU

An integrated GPU has no memory of its own. It borrows system RAM, which means memory bandwidth is graphics bandwidth. Run a single stick instead of two and you roughly halve that bandwidth, and the iGPU is the component that feels it first — this is a large part of why the EQ14's single-channel DDR4 configuration is called out as a limiter in reviews, on top of the UHD graphics being entry-level to begin with. The practical rule: if a mini PC has two SODIMM slots and you care at all about graphics, populate both. On boxes with soldered memory, like the Mac mini, the configuration you buy is the configuration you own — which is exactly why the base 16GB model deserves more scrutiny than a spec sheet with an upgrade path would.

Who should skip a mini PC

Three groups should think hard before buying one of these.

Gamers and anyone who wants a GPU upgrade path. Every machine here except the MS-01 runs integrated graphics with no way to add a discrete card, and the MS-01's slot is x8 electrically and physically limited to a low-profile card. The Radeon 780M in the SER8 and A8 Max is a capable iGPU for light gaming, but it is not a replacement for a desktop graphics card, and the N150 in the EQ14 isn't in the conversation at all — under 20 fps in CS2 at 1080p lowest, per published testing, is the whole story. If your plan involves a GPU upgrade in two years, buy a tower.

Anyone who upgrades storage or memory later. On Apple, RAM and SSD are soldered and permanent; the 256GB base model in particular is a decision you make once. The Windows boxes are better here — the EQ14's two M.2 slots and the MS-01's three are real advantages — but you're still working within the space a 1-litre chassis allows.

Anyone running heavy sustained loads. These are laptop chips in small enclosures. They will throttle under long renders and long compiles in a way a tower with a large air cooler won't, and the degree varies by how the vendor configured TDP and cooling. For bursty desktop work this is a non-issue. For an eight-hour render queue, it isn't.

One more honest note on evidence: independent coverage in this category is uneven. The Mac mini and the MS-01 have been reviewed to death; the specific 16GB/1TB EQ14 trim has less published testing behind it than the cheaper 512GB version most reviewers received. Where we've extrapolated from a nearby configuration, we've said so above rather than presenting it as a measured result. If you're cross-shopping against portable machines instead, our laptops under $1,000 guide covers the other side of that trade.

Bottom Line

For most people, the Mac mini M4 is the small computer that most completely replaces a desktop — buy it from Apple at $599 if you can, and seriously consider stepping up the storage, because you cannot fix that decision later. If you need Windows, the GEEKOM A8 Max is the better buy at the prices we observed, and the Beelink SER8 takes the crown the moment it returns to its historical price — so check both before you decide. If the box is going to sit in a cupboard running Home Assistant and a few containers, the Beelink EQ14 does that job for a third of the money and almost no electricity. And if you're building a home lab, nothing else at this size gives you dual 10G and a PCIe slot the way the Minisforum MS-01 does — just make sure you're buying the complete machine and not the barebone.
