How We Evaluate Gaming TVs

Loiter Point does not operate a testing lab. We do not own a colorimeter, a Leo Bodnar lag tester, or a light-sealed room, and we are not going to pretend otherwise while a competitor's measurement sits in an open browser tab.

What we do instead: we synthesize published independent measurements — primarily RTINGS, whose methodology is public, repeatable and applied consistently across every panel they buy — alongside verified owner reports from people running these panels with a PS5, an Xbox Series X or a PC every day. Where those two sources agree, we treat the finding as solid. Where they diverge, or where a number comes from a single secondary outlet, we say so in the text rather than laundering it into a confident-sounding spec.

Three practical consequences of that approach, worth stating up front:

1. Every real-world figure on this page is attributed. If a number does not have a source attached, it does not appear. That is also why some cells in our comparison table read "Not specified in sources we trust" — we would rather leave a hole than fill it from memory.

2. We do not quote a single hard price as current fact. TV pricing in 2026 is genuinely chaotic, and the LG C6 below is the case study: its 65" model went from roughly $2,699 list in mid-June 2026 to around $1,999 discounted, then to a reported low of $1,796.99 — a swing of roughly $900 in a matter of weeks. Any price we print is a snapshot of what a retailer showed at one moment. Check the live price.

3. We flag our own weak evidence. Where a figure comes from a secondary outlet rather than a top-tier measurement lab, we label it "reported" and tell you to treat it as indicative. That candor costs us nothing and it is the whole reason to read us instead of a listicle farm.

The Five Picks

01

Samsung S95H

QD-OLED flagship — best overall

Price context: Samsung lists the 65" (QN65S95HAFXZA) at $3,399.99. Treat that as a list-price anchor, not a promise — retailer discounting on flagship OLEDs is aggressive and unpredictable.

This is the one RTINGS rates as the best gaming TV they have tested overall, and their phrasing is unusually unhedged for a lab that hedges professionally: it delivers "with almost no compromises." That matters because most TVs at any price are a trade — great motion but mediocre brightness, or great brightness but crushed shadow detail. The S95H is the rare panel where the reviewers who measure this stuff for a living could not find the catch.

QD-OLED is the reason. You get OLED's per-pixel black levels and near-instant pixel response — which is what actually makes fast games look clean — with the quantum-dot color volume that conventional WOLED gives up. For a dark-room console setup, that combination is the current ceiling.

02

LG G6

OLED evo flagship — best for high-refresh PC

Price context: the 65" (OLED65G6WUA) has been seen from $2,999.99 at Abt up to $3,399.99 at Best Buy — a $400 spread on the same panel, on the same week. Shop it around.

The G6 is the pick if a gaming PC is plugged in alongside your console. It ships with a native 165Hz panel, which puts it past the 120Hz ceiling that consoles top out at and into territory where a PC running an older or well-optimized title can actually use the headroom. Every one of its four HDMI ports is 2.1 — see the nerd box below for why that specific detail is worth more than most spec-sheet bullet points.

It also supports Dolby Vision Gaming, which the Xbox Series X can drive at 120Hz, and LG's Game Optimizer gives you a single overlay for VRR state, genre presets and black-level tweaks without diving through the settings tree mid-match.

On input lag: a figure of 4.7ms has been reported running a PS5 in Performance Mode at 4K/120Hz with VRR enabled. We want to be straight with you about the provenance — that number comes from a secondary outlet rather than a top-tier measurement lab, so treat it as indicative rather than definitive. The honest takeaway is directional: the G6's lag is in the range where it stops being a factor, not that it is precisely 4.7 milliseconds.

03

Hisense U8QG

Mid-range — best bright-room value

Price context: the 100-inch model is priced at $2,997.96. That is the 100" figure specifically — do not read it as the price of a 65". Normal sizes sell for substantially less, and we do not have verified figures for them, so check the live price.

An RTINGS Gaming score of 8.3/10 puts the U8QG within striking distance of panels costing several times as much, and it gets there without the OLED tax. Like the G6, it runs a native 165Hz at 4K — that is unusual at this tier and it is the single spec that most justifies the pick for anyone with a PC in the mix.

The differentiator versus the OLEDs above is the Anti-Glare Low Reflection Pro panel. If your setup is a living room with windows — the scenario where OLED's contrast advantage evaporates and reflections become the actual problem — this is arguably the more sensible buy regardless of budget. It also carries a very wide color gamut, which is what keeps HDR games from looking washed out on a non-OLED.

04

TCL QM8

Budget-to-mid — best HDR format coverage

Price context: we do not have a verified current price for the QM8 from a source we trust, and we are not going to invent one. It sits in the budget-to-mid bracket; check the live listing.

The QM8 matches the Hisense at 8.3/10 for Gaming from RTINGS while sitting a tier lower on price, which is the entire argument for it. Its headline gaming feature is a 288Hz refresh rate at 1080p — worth being precise about what that means. No console will use it. It is a PC feature, aimed at competitive shooters where players deliberately drop resolution to chase frame rate. If that is not you, it is a spec you will never touch.

More broadly useful is HDR ULTRA, covering Dolby Vision IQ, HDR10+ and HLG. Broad format support means you are not gambling on which HDR standard a given game or streaming app decided to ship — the panel handles whichever arrives.

LG C6

OLED value pick — and the only one we can link directly

Price context: the 65" went from roughly $2,699 list in mid-June 2026 to around $1,999 discounted, and then to a reported low of $1,796.99. That is roughly a $900 swing in weeks on one model. Do not buy this at list price without checking whether a sale is running.

The C-series has been the default "OLED for people who did not want to spend flagship money" recommendation for years, and the C6 keeps that job. It has HDMI 2.1 on all four inputs plus VRR and ALLM, which is the full console feature set — a PS5 and a Series X can both run at their maximum capability and the TV will switch to low-latency mode on its own when it sees a game signal.

It also has the widest size range here: 42, 48, 55, 65, 77 and 83 inches. The 42" and 48" matter more than they look — those are the sizes that work as a desk monitor for a PC, and almost nothing else on this list goes that small.

We are not going to attach a refresh-rate number to the C6 because we do not have one from a source we trust. Nerd box — why "HDMI 2.1 on ALL ports" is the spec that actually bites

Manufacturers love to write "HDMI 2.1" on a box when exactly one of the four ports supports it. That is technically true and practically useless, and here is the arithmetic that catches people out.

Count your devices. A PS5 needs a full-bandwidth port to run 4K/120Hz with VRR. An Xbox Series X needs the same. If you own both, that is two ports gone. Now add a soundbar or AV receiver: unless you are willing to run audio over optical and lose lossless formats, you need eARC, and on most TVs eARC lives on one specific HDMI port — frequently one of the good ones. A gaming PC makes three high-bandwidth devices. On a TV with two 2.1 ports and eARC sharing one of them, you are already unplugging cables by hand, or buying a switch that introduces its own handshake problems.

This is why the LG G6 and LG C6 earn a specific mention for having 2.1 on all four inputs. It is not a bragging spec. It is the difference between a clean install and a permanently annoying one.

The related point: past a certain threshold, input lag stops being the interesting number. Human reaction time to a visual stimulus lives in the hundreds of milliseconds. A difference between, say, 5ms and 9ms is well under a single frame at 120Hz and is not something a player perceives as lag. What people do perceive is judder and tearing — which is a VRR and refresh-rate problem, not a latency one. So when you compare these TVs, weight "does it run 120Hz with VRR reliably on the ports I need" far above chasing a lag figure a millisecond lower. That is also why we are relaxed about the G6's 4.7ms number being from a secondary source: even if the real figure is meaningfully higher, the conclusion does not move.

Bottom Line

If budget is genuinely not the constraint, buy the Samsung S95H. When the lab that measures everything says a TV is their best gaming panel "with almost no compromises," the decision is made for you. Samsung's own $3,399.99 listing for the 65" tells you what that verdict costs.

If your primary machine is a gaming PC, the LG G6 is the more targeted buy — native 165Hz, HDMI 2.1 on all four ports, Dolby Vision Gaming. The $400 spread between retailers on the same panel is free money if you spend ten minutes shopping it.

If you want most of the gaming performance for a fraction of the flagship outlay, the Hisense U8QG and TCL QM8 both land at 8.3/10 on RTINGS' Gaming score. Choose the Hisense if your room is bright — that anti-glare panel is the deciding feature. Choose the TCL if you are on a PC and price is the binding constraint.

And if you want an OLED without flagship pricing, the LG C6 is the pragmatic answer, with six sizes and full HDMI 2.1 across all four inputs. Just do not buy it on a whim: a roughly $900 swing on the 65" in a matter of weeks means the difference between a good purchase and a bad one is entirely a matter of timing. Check the live price on the day, on more than one retailer, and be willing to wait.

Last thing, said plainly: nothing on this page comes from a lab we run. It comes from reading measurements taken by people who do run labs, cross-checking them against owners living with these panels, and refusing to print anything we cannot point at a source for. Where the evidence was thin — the C6's refresh rate, the G6's exact input lag, the QM8's street price — we told you so instead of smoothing it over.
