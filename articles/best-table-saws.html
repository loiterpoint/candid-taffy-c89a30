How We Evaluate

Loiter Point does not operate a woodshop bench or a cut-test rig. What we do is read the outlets that run their own hands-on testing — Pro Tool Reviews, Family Handyman, This Old House, Bob Vila — cross-check their findings against a large pool of owner reviews, and pull out the models that keep landing on lists for a defensible reason. When a number comes from a manufacturer's spec sheet, we tag it (rated). When it comes from someone who measured or used the saw, we tag it (reported). We do not blend the two.

Roles matter more than a single winner. A 90-pound rolling jobsite saw is the wrong tool for a fixed woodshop, and a 400-pound cabinet saw is the wrong tool for the back of a truck. So the five picks below are split by job: an overall jobsite saw for most buyers, a safety-first portable, a budget pick, a cordless option, and a stationary cabinet saw for a home shop.

On price: street prices in this category move week to week and swing hard around holiday tool sales. The figures here are approximate street prices observed in mid-July 2026 and are there for sizing the decision, not quoting a receipt. Check the retailer before you buy. Portable jobsite saws in particular are frequently bundled or discounted, and cabinet-saw pricing shifts with fence and extension-table options.

The Picks

01 — Best Overall Jobsite
DeWalt DWE7491RSDEFAULT PICK
~$649

The DWE7491RS is the model that shows up most often as the "buy this if you're not sure" jobsite saw, and the reasoning is consistent across outlets. Pro Tool Reviews and Bob Vila both keep it near the top of their lists, and Bob Vila's testing called out its precision and portability specifically. The headline feature reviewers return to is the rack-and-pinion telescoping fence: it racks in and out smoothly and holds parallel to the blade without the fiddling that cheaper T-square fences demand (reported). Its 32.5-inch rip capacity is the widest on any portable saw, which is what lets it break down a full sheet of plywood without an outfeed dance.

DeWalt rates it at a 15-amp motor spinning to 4,800 RPM, with a 3-1/8-inch depth of cut at 90 degrees on a 10-inch blade (rated). It ships with the rolling stand, which reviewers consistently rate as one of the better fold-and-roll designs — it sets up and breaks down fast and stays stable once deployed (reported). The saw includes a modular guard, a riving knife, and anti-kickback pawls. It does not have a blade brake; if flesh-detection safety is your priority, the SawStop below is the saw to read next. The whole unit runs about 90 pounds with the stand.

02 — Best Safety / Premium Portable
SawStop Jobsite Saw Pro (JSS-120A60)BLADE BRAKE
~$1,599

SawStop's whole reason for existing is one feature: an active safety system that senses contact between skin and a spinning blade, then fires a spring-loaded aluminum cartridge into the teeth and drops the blade below the table in under 5 milliseconds. The system works by running a small electrical signal through the blade and watching for the change that conductive material — a finger — causes. Pro Tool Reviews reviewed this exact model and framed it as a better jobsite saw that also happens to carry the brake, not a safety gimmick bolted onto a mediocre saw (reported). It is the only portable pick here with that technology.

SawStop rates it as a 1.5-HP, 15-amp, 120V saw with a 25.5-inch rip capacity on a 10-inch blade (rated), on a mobile cart with a T-style fence. The cost of the safety system is real and worth understanding before you buy: any activation — a real finger strike or a false trigger from wet or conductive stock — destroys both the brake cartridge and usually the blade, and the cartridge runs roughly $70 to replace (see the nerd box on brake economics below). The rip capacity is narrower than the DeWalt's, and the price is more than double. What you pay for is the brake.

03 — Best Value / Budget
Skil TS6307-00BUDGET
~$330

The TS6307-00 is the pick for buyers who want a real saw — rack-and-pinion fence, riving knife, folding stand — without paying jobsite-pro money. Its selling point in reviews is that it borrows features that used to be reserved for saws twice its price: the rack-and-pinion fence racks smoothly and stays square, and reviewers note the blade guard is less cumbersome than most budget guards, so beginners are more likely to leave it on (reported). At roughly 51 pounds on a folding stand, it sets up quickly and stores small.

Skil rates it at a 15-amp motor turning 4,800 RPM with a 25.5-inch rip capacity on a 10-inch blade (rated). The clear limit, per owner reports, is thick hardwood: it rips oak and maple fine at a patient feed rate, but it is not built for production ripping of inch-and-a-half stock, and you will feel it slow down (reported). It carries a proper riving knife, blade guard, and anti-kickback pawls, but no blade brake. As a first table saw or a light-duty second saw, it does more than its price suggests.

04 — Best Cordless
Milwaukee M18 FUEL 8-1/4" (2736-21HD)CORDLESS
~$749 kit

Cordless table saws used to be a compromise. The M18 FUEL 8-1/4" is the model reviewers stopped apologizing for: after side-by-side cut tests, Pro Tool Reviews concluded it cuts like a corded saw and edged out the cordless DeWalt for the category lead (reported). For a contractor already invested in M18 batteries, or anyone framing and cutting on a site with no convenient outlet, losing the cord is worth real money. A POWERSTATE brushless motor and One-Key electronics handle the load, and the kit ships with the HD12.0 high-output pack that does the heavy lifting.

Milwaukee rates it at 6,300 RPM with a 24.5-inch rip capacity on an 8-1/4-inch blade, and quotes up to roughly 600 linear feet of cutting per charge on the included battery (rated). Two trade-offs come with the format: the 8-1/4-inch blade means a shallower maximum depth of cut than the 10-inch saws above, and battery cost is baked into the price — the kit lands well above the corded DeWalt. If you never leave a garage with outlets, a corded saw does the same work for less. It carries a riving knife, guard, and pawls, and no blade brake. At about 41 pounds it is the lightest pick here.

When the saw lives in a fixed shop rather than a truck bed, the calculus changes: mass, a cast-iron top, a real fence, and enclosed dust collection start to matter more than fold-and-roll portability. The 1.75-HP Professional Cabinet Saw is the model Pro Tool Reviews and others reach for as the home-shop cabinet default, and it pairs that stationary build with the same flesh-detecting brake as the jobsite SawStop above — the blade stops and drops in under 5 milliseconds on skin contact (rated). What to Know Before You Buy

// Nerd Box — Jobsite vs Hybrid vs Cabinet, and What Rip Capacity Buys You

The three classes are really three trade-offs between portability and cutting mass. A jobsite saw (DeWalt, Skil, Milwaukee here) is light, folds onto a stand, and runs a direct-drive universal motor — you carry it, and it rips sheet goods and framing lumber. A cabinet saw (the SawStop PCS) is a several-hundred-pound stationary machine with a cast-iron top and an enclosed base, built for accuracy, vibration damping, and dust collection; it does not move. A hybrid saw splits the difference — cabinet-style build on a lighter frame, often on a 120V circuit — for shops that want stationary accuracy without cabinet-saw mass or a 240V line.

Rip capacity is the distance from the blade to the fence at its widest — it sets the largest panel you can rip in one pass with the fence guiding it. A full sheet of plywood is 48 inches wide; a saw with 32.5 inches of rip can cut a sheet in half down the middle with the offcut supported, while 24 to 25 inches means you reposition or rough-cut oversize panels first. More rip capacity is convenience and safety on wide stock, not raw power. It says nothing about how thick a board the saw can cut — that is depth of cut, set by blade diameter.

// Nerd Box — Blade-Brake Economics and the Riving Knife

SawStop's brake is the reason two picks here cost what they do, so the running cost is worth spelling out. The system fires only on a detected flesh contact — or a false trigger from wet, green, or otherwise conductive stock, or from a stray piece of metal. When it fires, the aluminum brake cartridge is consumed and the blade is almost always ruined by the stop. Budget roughly $70 for a replacement cartridge plus the cost of a new blade per activation, and keep a spare cartridge on the shelf so a trigger does not end your day. There is a bypass mode for deliberately cutting conductive material with the brake disabled. No competing brand ships this system; SawStop's original patents have begun to expire, but as of mid-2026 it remains the flesh-detection option on the market.

Every saw here — brake or not — ships with a riving knife, and it is the single most important standard safety feature to keep installed. A riving knife is a curved metal plate that sits just behind the blade, rises and falls with it, and keeps the saw kerf from pinching closed on the back of the blade — the pinch that throws stock back at the operator (kickback). Unlike an old-style splitter, it moves with the blade and works on non-through cuts too. The blade guard and anti-kickback pawls stack on top of it. A blade brake stops a cut that has already gone wrong; the riving knife helps stop it from going wrong in the first place, which is why buyers who skip everything else should not skip this.

For after-cut cleanup of a car or a workspace, a compact car vacuum handles the light stuff a shop vac is overkill for.

Bottom Line

For most buyers, the DeWalt DWE7491RS is the safe default jobsite saw: the widest rip capacity in its class, a fence that stays square, and a rolling stand, with no blade brake as the only real omission. If finger safety outranks everything, the SawStop Jobsite Saw Pro brings the flesh-detection brake to a portable body — accept the higher price and the cartridge-plus-blade cost after any trigger. The Skil TS6307-00 is the value entry point, the Milwaukee M18 FUEL is worth its premium only if you are on M18 batteries or work away from outlets, and the SawStop PCS175 is the buy-once cabinet saw for a fixed home shop.
