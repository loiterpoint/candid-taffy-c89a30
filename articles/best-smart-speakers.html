What actually changed in 2026

If you last bought a smart speaker two or three years ago, almost every recommendation you remember is now obsolete. Three things happened in quick succession.

Google deleted its lineup and started over. The Nest Mini and Nest Audio have been discontinued, and Google replaced its entire consumer speaker range with one model: the Google Home Speaker, released June 25, 2026 at $99.99. That speaker also marks the handover from Google Assistant to Gemini for Home, ending a six-year run for the Assistant brand. Existing Nest hardware keeps working and receives Gemini through a software update — you are not being orphaned — but there is now exactly one Google speaker to buy, at one price. Simplicity, or a narrowing of choice, depending on your mood.

Amazon rebuilt Echo around Alexa+. In late 2025 Amazon replaced its lineup with hardware designed natively for Alexa+: the Echo Dot Max, the redesigned Echo Studio, and the Echo Show 8 and Show 11. These run on new AZ3 and AZ3 Pro silicon with a dedicated on-device AI accelerator, plus a sensor stack Amazon calls Omnisense. The practical upshot is that the conversational assistant features stop feeling bolted on. New Echo buyers get Alexa+ out of the box.

Apple missed the window. As of July 2026 the HomePod mini 2 has not launched. Reporting points to a slip to roughly September 2026, arriving alongside the revamped Siri in iOS 27. That timing has a direct consequence for what you should buy today, and we get to it below.

The one rule that beats every spec

The single most important decision in this category is which assistant ecosystem you are already in — and it matters more than audio specs, driver counts or wattage. A speaker that sounds 15% better but can't dim the lights you already own is the worse purchase, every time. Work out whether your home is Alexa, Google or Apple first. Then, and only then, argue about tweeters.

How We Evaluate Smart Speakers

Loiter Point does not run an anechoic chamber, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. There are outlets that do own measurement rigs — and there are thousands of people living with these speakers in real rooms. Our job is to synthesize both into a recommendation you can act on.

Here's the process behind every pick on this page:

  • Published independent testing. We collect reviews from outlets that do hands-on evaluation and, where possible, instrumented measurement — Engadget, Consumer Reports, TechRadar, SoundGuys, Trusted Reviews, Digital Trends, Android Central, HotHardware, CNN Underscored, Forbes and others — and weight them by how much of the review is grounded in observation rather than a spec sheet.
  • Verified owner reports. Aggregate retail ratings, long-term owner threads and post-firmware-update complaints. This is where you catch the problems that only surface at month three, which is exactly the window most launch reviews miss.
  • Manufacturer documentation. Rated specs come from the vendors themselves — blog.google, aboutamazon.com, spec pages. We treat these as claims, not conclusions. Rated is what the box says. Real-world is what independent reviewers and owners report.
  • Disagreement gets published, not smoothed over. When credible reviewers reach opposite conclusions — which is exactly what happened with Echo Dot Max sound quality — we show you the split rather than picking whichever side makes for a cleaner verdict. A guide that never admits uncertainty is a guide that isn't paying attention.

Every "est. real-world" or "reported" figure in the spec grids below is a synthesis of that published evidence. It is never a Loiter Point measurement, because we have no instrument to make one with.

Nerd box

Why a 2.5-inch woofer can't give you real bass, no matter whose DSP is driving it

Every compact smart speaker in this guide runs into the same wall, and it isn't a software problem. To reproduce low frequencies at a given loudness, a driver has to move a certain volume of air — that quantity is displacement, roughly cone area multiplied by how far the cone travels. Cone area scales with the square of the radius, so shrinking a woofer punishes you hard: halve the diameter and you have roughly a quarter of the area to work with.

It gets worse as you go lower. For constant sound pressure, required excursion rises steeply as frequency falls — dropping an octave demands dramatically more cone travel for the same output. A 2.5-inch woofer in a small sealed box simply runs out of physical room to move before it reaches the bottom octaves, and pushing it further produces distortion rather than bass.

The sealed enclosure adds its own constraint. Trapped air behind the cone acts as a spring; the smaller the box, the stiffer that spring, which raises the system's resonant frequency and rolls off the low end above it. This is why enclosure volume, not driver quality, is usually the binding constraint in a puck-sized speaker — and why the Sonos Era 100 and the full-size HomePod, both physically larger, extend lower than anything at $99.99.

DSP can help at the margins — equalization, dynamic bass boost, psychoacoustic tricks that imply a fundamental your speaker never actually produced. What it cannot do is manufacture displacement. When you read that the Echo Dot Max has bass depth limited by enclosure size, that is physics being reported, not a design failure.

Nerd box

On-device NPU inference vs. the cloud round trip

The most consequential hardware shift in this generation isn't acoustic — it's that both Amazon and Google now ship dedicated AI silicon inside a hundred-dollar speaker. Amazon's AZ3 and AZ3 Pro include an on-device AI accelerator; the Google Home Speaker carries an NPU running Gemini Nano locally, which Google frames as enabling sub-100ms responses.

Why that matters comes down to where the work happens. A classic cloud assistant does something like: wake word detected locally → audio encoded and uploaded → speech recognition in a datacenter → intent parsing → response generated → audio streamed back → playback. Every arrow crosses a network. You pay for DNS, TLS setup, upload time, queueing at the server, and the return trip — and you pay it again for the next turn of the conversation. Even on good broadband, the network legs alone dominate the perceived delay.

Running inference on the device deletes the arrows. Nothing leaves the house for the common cases, so there's no upload, no queue and no return trip — which is how you get to a response time short enough that a person stops perceiving it as a wait at all. It's also why local processing tends to hold up when your connection is congested or briefly drops.

There's a second, quieter benefit: audio that's processed locally doesn't need to be sent anywhere to be understood. How much of that actually applies depends on each vendor's specific implementation and settings — treat it as a structural advantage of the architecture rather than a blanket privacy guarantee. And note that heavier requests still reach for the cloud on all of these devices. The NPU handles the fast, frequent things well; it isn't running the whole model.

How to choose

Start with the ecosystem, seriously

We put this up top and we'll repeat it here because it's the recommendation most often ignored: whichever assistant already controls your lights, locks, thermostat and phone should pick your speaker. A speaker that can't talk to the devices you own is a Bluetooth speaker with extra steps. If you're Google, the Google Home Speaker is effectively your only new option — and the Sonos Era 100 is off the table entirely, since it doesn't support Google Assistant.

Then decide whether you need a hub

The Echo Dot Max and Echo Studio both include a built-in smart home hub. The Sonos Era 100 does not, and its automation depth trails native speakers accordingly. If you're building out smart home devices, that built-in hub is worth more than it looks on a spec sheet — it's one less box, one less power outlet and one less thing to troubleshoot.

Only then argue about sound

If sound is genuinely your priority and you're not locked to Google, the Era 100 is the reported leader among compact models. If you want spatial audio inside Alexa, the Echo Studio. If you're Apple and already committed, the full-size HomePod. And if you want the most competence per dollar, the Dot Max — with the caveat, stated again, that reviewers disagree about how good it actually sounds.

Timing matters this year

Two timing notes. First, don't buy a HomePod mini before the mini 2 lands around September 2026. Second, the Era 100's street price has swung more than $100 in 2026 — if you're flexible on when you buy, waiting for a sale is worth real money on that one specifically.

Common questions

My Nest Mini still works. Do I have to replace it?

No. Google discontinued the Nest Mini and Nest Audio, but existing units keep working and receive Gemini through a software update. You are getting the new assistant without buying new hardware. Upgrade for the audio improvement if you want it — not out of fear of being cut off.

Do I need to pay for Alexa+?

New Echo buyers get Alexa+ out of the box on the current hardware, which includes the Echo Dot Max and the 2025 Echo Studio. That's part of why the current generation is the one to buy if you're going Amazon.

Is the Google Home Speaker worth it without the subscription?

It's a competent $99.99 speaker regardless. But multiple reviewers flag that the most compelling Gemini features are paywalled, so check what's included in the free tier against what actually attracted you. If the answer is "the thing I wanted costs extra every month," factor that into the price.

What if I don't want microphones in my house?

Sonos sells the Era 100 SL, a mic-free variant — same speaker, microphones removed rather than software-disabled. You lose voice control entirely and drive it from the app or AirPlay 2 instead. For privacy-minded buyers that's often the right trade.

Should I just wait for something better?

Generally no — Amazon and Google both refreshed within the last nine months, so you're at the start of a cycle, not the end of one. The exception is Apple's mini, where waiting is the clear call.

Bottom Line

The 2026 market is simpler than it looks, because the ecosystem question answers most of it for you. Alexa households should buy the Echo Dot Max at $99.99 and step up to the Echo Studio at $219.99 if they want spatial audio and a bigger main-room sound. Google households have exactly one new option, the Google Home Speaker at $99.99 — a real improvement over Nest Audio, with a subscription asterisk worth checking first. If sound is the whole point and you're not on Google, the Sonos Era 100 leads the compact class on reported frequency response and bass extension — verify today's price, because it has ranged from $179 to $289 this year. Apple households that need something now should get the full-size HomePod at $349, and should not touch the HomePod mini until the mini 2 arrives around September.
