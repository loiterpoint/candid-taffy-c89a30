A home NAS solves three problems at once: it gives every machine in the house a backup target, it gets your photo library off a cloud subscription and onto hardware you own, and it feeds Plex or Jellyfin without a laptop being left awake. The hard part of buying one in 2026 isn't finding capable hardware — it's that the four-figure decision is really about software, networking, and platform policy, none of which appear on the spec sheet in a useful way.

This roundup picks five boxes across roughly $300 to $700, all sold diskless. Read the budget section at the bottom before you decide how much box you can afford, because drives usually cost as much as the NAS itself.

How We Evaluate Home NAS Boxes

Loiter Point does not run a lab, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. We do not own these units, we have not benchmarked them, and any number in this article that describes real-world behaviour is attributed to somebody who did the work.

What we actually do: we synthesise published independent measurements and reviews, manufacturer specifications, and verified owner reports from support forums and retail feedback, then cross-check them against each other. Where those sources disagree — and on NAS transfer speeds they frequently do, because results depend heavily on the switch, the client NIC, and the drives fitted — we say so rather than picking the flattering number. Interface ceilings (1GbE, 2.5GbE, 10GbE) are quoted as theoretical maximums, not results. Spec rows marked "est. real-world" or "reported" are exactly that: estimates and third-party reports, never our measurements.

We weight four things: the maturity of the software platform, because you live in it daily; networking headroom, because it is the most common bottleneck; transcoding capability, because it decides whether Plex works for remote users; and total cost including drives. We also weight vendor policy history, which is why the Synology section below is longer than the rest.

Nerd Box: The Synology Drive-Lock Saga

In early 2025 Synology introduced a drive-compatibility policy that made third-party hard drives — Seagate IronWolf, WD Red Plus, the standard NAS drives everyone owns — effectively unusable in its 2025-generation models, including the DS925+, DS1825+ and DS425+. The unit would refuse to complete setup with an unverified drive. Not a warning banner: a hard stop.

After significant backlash, Synology reversed course in DSM 7.3, released October 2025. Third-party HDDs and 2.5" SATA SSDs are now accepted on those models without warnings or restrictions. The caveat that still applies, and that buyers keep missing: the reversal does not extend to M.2 NVMe drives. Creating an M.2 storage pool still requires drives from Synology's official compatibility list, so if you were planning to drop a pair of cheap consumer NVMe sticks into the DS925+ for caching, check the list first.

We are including this history because the fix does not make the episode irrelevant. Platform policy — what the vendor can decide about your hardware after you have bought it, via a firmware update — is part of what you are purchasing. Synology reversed this one under pressure, which is a reasonable sign. It is still worth knowing the policy existed. (Sources: Tom's Hardware, Synology Knowledge Center, NASCompares.)

Nerd Box: Networking Is Usually the Real Bottleneck

Interface ceilings, as theoretical maximums rather than measured results: 1GbE tops out around 110 MB/s, 2.5GbE around 280 MB/s, and 10GbE around 1.1 GB/s. You only see those numbers if every link in the chain keeps up — the switch, the client's NIC, the cabling, and critically the drives themselves.

That last one is where 10GbE marketing gets ahead of reality. A four-drive array of spinning disks will typically saturate 2.5GbE comfortably but will not come close to filling a 10GbE pipe. Ten-gigabit mainly pays off when you add NVMe caching or run an all-SSD array. Buying a 10GbE NAS and plugging it into a 1GbE switch buys you nothing at all — check your switch and your client machine before you pay for the port. If your network is the weak link, start with our guides to Wi-Fi routers and mesh Wi-Fi systems, and remember that Wi-Fi 6E or 7 clients will still be slower to the NAS than a wired 2.5GbE link.

What Else You Need to Budget For

Drives. Every box in this article ships diskless. This is the single most common budgeting mistake — filling four bays with NAS-rated drives often costs as much as the NAS itself, sometimes more, and it means the real entry price of a 4-bay build is well north of $1,000. Decide your capacity target first, then work backwards to the chassis you can afford. Buy NAS-rated drives; desktop drives in a 24/7 vibrating four-bay array are a false economy.

A UPS. Strongly recommended, not optional in our view. A NAS is a computer that is always on, always holding dirty write caches, and running a filesystem that does not enjoy sudden power loss. Every OS here supports graceful shutdown triggered by a USB-connected UPS. A basic line-interactive unit is cheap insurance against a rebuild — see our UPS and battery backup picks for sizing.

An actual backup. RAID is not a backup. RAID protects against a drive failing; it does nothing about ransomware, a bad delete, a failed controller, a fire, or a theft. Follow 3-2-1: three copies of the data, on two different media types, with one copy off-site. In practice that means the NAS plus a cloud target or a rotating portable SSD you keep somewhere else. Synology's Hyper Backup and standard tools like restic or rclone all handle this.

Bottom Line

For most households the UGREEN NASync DXP4800 Plus is the right buy: four bays, an iGPU that makes Plex behave, dual M.2, and 10GbE you can grow into, at a price where competitors give you 2.5GbE and a slower chip. The cost is a younger software platform.

If you would rather the appliance be boring and dependable, buy the Synology DS925+ and accept that hardware transcoding is off the table — just go in knowing the 2025 drive-policy history and the M.2 compatibility-list caveat that survived the reversal. On a tighter budget, the UGREEN DXP2800 is the value play and the Synology DS224+ is the simplest possible on-ramp if you can live with 1GbE. And if you want maximum compute and are comfortable managing a less mature platform yourself, the TerraMaster F4-424 Pro gives you eight cores and 32GB for the money.

Whichever you pick: budget for the drives, put it behind a UPS, and set up an off-box backup on day one. The hardware choice matters far less than doing those three things.
