OLED is no longer a question of whether the picture is good. Every set on this page produces the same fundamentally correct image: per-pixel light control, black that is genuinely black rather than dark grey, and near-instantaneous pixel response that makes motion look clean. The differences between them come down to three things — how bright the panel gets, what the screen coating does to the light in your actual room, and how much you pay for the privilege.

That last point matters more in 2026 than it usually does. The headline finding above is not our editorializing; it is RTINGS' stated position, and it reframes the whole category. If the 2026 sets are only marginally better than the 2025 sets, and the 2025 sets are now heavily discounted, then the "best" OLED for a lot of buyers is a last-generation one. We have built this guide around that reality rather than around release dates.

How We Evaluate OLED TVs

Loiter Point does not run a test lab, and we do not evaluate these TVs first-hand. There are outlets that do this properly — with darkened rooms, colorimeters, pattern generators and repeatable procedures — and pretending to duplicate that work would be dishonest.

What we do instead: we synthesize published independent lab measurements, primarily RTINGS, alongside verified owner reports from people living with these sets in ordinary rooms. Every performance claim on this page is attributed to its source. Where a figure comes from a manufacturer or retailer listing rather than an independent lab, we say so.

We also state when the evidence is thin. Several specifications on this page — particularly for the Samsung S90H — are only available from retail listings, and we have marked those as unconfirmed rather than filling the gap with a plausible-sounding number. Prices are the least stable data on this page; treat every one of them as a snapshot, not a quote.

Nerd Box — QD-OLED vs WOLED, and what matte vs glossy actually changes

Two different OLED panel technologies are in play here. Samsung's S95H and S90H use QD-OLED; LG's C6, C5 and B5 use WOLED. Both give you per-pixel illumination and the same true black floor. Where they diverge, based on RTINGS' assessments, is in color and brightness character: RTINGS describes the QD-OLED S95H as having bright, vibrant colors and very high peak brightness that holds up even when most of the screen is lit — and specifically notes that the older-panel C6 sizes don't produce colors as vivid as the S95H.

The screen coating is the part people underestimate, and it's the more practical decision. The S95H is matte; the C6 and B5 are glossy. RTINGS credits the S95H's anti-reflective coating with effectively eliminating glare from lamps and windows, and separately notes that the C6's glossy coating makes glare more distracting during the day. The physical trade-off is real and well understood: matte diffuses reflected light across the panel rather than bouncing a sharp mirror image at you.

Practical rule: if you can control the light in your room — blinds, evening viewing, no lamp behind the couch — glossy is fine and you should spend the money on something else. If your TV faces a window, the matte panel is the upgrade worth paying for, and it's the strongest argument for the S95H over anything else in this guide.

Nerd Box — the LG C6 panel lottery, by screen size

This one is easy to get wrong and expensive to get wrong. RTINGS reports that the LG C6 does not use the same panel across every size in the range.

The 42", 55" and 65" C6 use an older panel type, and RTINGS notes their colors aren't as vivid as the S95H. The 48" C6 — and the 77" and 83" LG C6H — use a newer panel with more vibrant colors. So the 48-inch model is, counter-intuitively, running better panel technology than the 65-inch model sitting next to it in the store.

What to do with that: if you're choosing between a 48" and a 55", you're not just choosing size — you're choosing a color performance tier, and the smaller one wins on panel. If you're set on 65", know that you're on the older panel and price your decision accordingly; that's a point in favour of buying a discounted C5 instead and pocketing the difference. And if you want a very large LG with the newer panel, the 77" and 83" C6H are the models to search for, not the plain C6.

Bottom Line

The honest summary of OLED in 2026 is that it's a good year to be a patient buyer and a bad year to be an early adopter. RTINGS' own framing — that 2026 models are expensive near release, that most don't improve much on their 2025 predecessors, and that now is a great time to buy a discounted 2025 OLED — is the most valuable thing on this page, and it happens to point away from the most expensive products.

So: if your room is bright and budget isn't the constraint, buy the Samsung S95H, because the matte coating solves a problem no amount of brightness alone can. If you want the best balance of price and performance in current hardware, buy the LG C6 — and check the panel lottery box before you commit to a size. If you want the most sensible purchase on this page, buy the discounted LG C5 and spend the savings on a soundbar. And if you're getting into OLED for the first time in a dim room, the LG B5 gets you the blacks that made the technology famous.

Whatever you choose, confirm the price yourself before you click. Every figure on this page is a snapshot of July 2026, and OLED pricing moves faster than almost anything else in consumer tech.
