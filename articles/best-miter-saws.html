How We Evaluate

Loiter Point does not run a tool lab. We do not clamp saws to a torsion box, chase runout with a dial indicator, or count cuts per charge in a heated shop. Other outlets do that work, and they do it well. Our job is to read across their published testing and owner reports, line the results up against maker specifications, and separate what has been measured from what is merely printed on the box.

Throughout this guide, capacity and range figures marked (rated) come from the manufacturer — crosscut width, bevel and miter range, motor amperage. Figures marked (reported) come from a named independent reviewer who put the saw on a bench: cut accuracy, runtime, how hard the motor works in thick stock. When the two disagree, we say so. We do not publish numbers we cannot trace to a source.

Prices are street prices as observed in mid-July 2026, rounded to the nearest useful figure. Miter saws move on promotion constantly — DeWalt's DWS780 has swung between roughly $449 and $669 in a single season, and holiday and spring tool events cut deeper. Treat every price here as a checkpoint, not a quote. Verify at the retailer before you buy.

The Picks

Best Overall — 12" Slider
DeWalt DWS780EDITORS' CONSENSUS ~$449

The DWS780 is the corded 12-inch double-bevel sliding saw that keeps landing at or near the top of independent roundups. Tech Gear Lab calls it the most capable saw it tested, a well-refined tool built for big jobs; Family Handyman singles out the XPS shadow-line system, which throws a real shadow of the blade onto the workpiece so the cut line is visible without a laser to calibrate. Reviewers repeatedly note the saw arrives close to square, and several report crosscut accuracy inside half a degree without adjustment (reported).

Rated crosscut is up to 2x14 dimensional lumber at 90 degrees, with bevel stops both directions and a 60-degree miter to the right (rated). It is a heavy, corded, shop-anchored machine — this is not the saw you carry up a ladder — but as a fixed station for trim, framing, and general dimensional work, it is the reference point the others get measured against.

Best Space-Saver — Axial Glide
Bosch GCM12SDSMALL-SHOP ~$549

Bosch replaced the two rear rails of a conventional slider with a hinged, articulating arm it calls Axial-Glide. The practical payoff is wall clearance: where a rail saw needs to slide its rails back 24 inches or more, the GCM12SD's arm folds in place, so Bosch rates it to run against a wall with roughly a foot behind it. In a garage or a crowded shop, that difference decides whether the saw fits at all. Pro Tool Reviews names it their best overall pick and credits the glide action as smooth and precise (reported).

Rated capacity is 14 inches horizontal, 6-1/2 inches vertical against the fence, and 6-1/2 inches of nested crown at 45 degrees, driven by a 15-amp motor at 4,000 rpm (rated). The tradeoff is weight and price — the glide mechanism is heavier and dearer than a rail slider of the same capacity — and the arm wants an occasional check for play as it ages. For a bench that lives against a wall, it earns the premium.

Best Cordless — 12" FlexVolt
DeWalt DCS781JOBSITE ~$849 (kit)

The DCS781 is the current-generation FlexVolt 12-inch double-bevel slider, and its headline change is that it runs off a single 60V battery instead of the two the previous cordless model needed. Pro Tool Reviews and Men's Journal both report it powering through pressure-treated 4x4s, cedar posts, and Douglas fir for nearly two working days on one 9.0Ah pack (reported); DeWalt rates it to roughly 675 cuts in base molding or 255 cuts in a 4x4 per charge (rated). Regenerative braking feeds some stopping energy back to the battery, which is part of how the runtime holds up.

Crosscut capacity mirrors the corded 12-inch saws at 2x14 at 90 degrees (rated), and the bare tool has slimmed to just over 50 pounds versus the nearly 69 pounds of the older cordless (reported). It is not cheap — the kit lands near $849, the bare tool near $699 — but for framing, deck, and fence work away from an outlet, it removes the cord without giving up 12-inch reach.

Best Compact Slider — 10"
Makita LS1019LTIGHT SPACES ~$599

Makita's answer to the wall-clearance problem is different from Bosch's: it runs the slide rails alongside the head rather than out the back, so the LS1019L keeps a small footprint while still sliding. Reviewers describe a 10-inch saw whose cutting capacity rivals many 12-inch models — up to 4-3/4 inches of baseboard vertical and 6-5/8 inches of nested crown, enough to cut 4x4 in a single pass (rated). The 15-amp direct-drive motor has a soft start, which several reviewers credit for smoother, more controlled cuts (reported).

It is a finish-carpentry saw first: base, casing, crown, hardwood parts, and dimensional lumber that still needs to travel. The included laser marks the kerf, and dual dust ports feed a bag or a vacuum. At around $599 it costs as much as some 12-inch saws, so the case for it is the compact geometry, not the price — if wall clearance and portability matter more than raw 12-inch reach, this is the saw.

Best Value — 10" Slider
Skil MS6305-00BUDGET ~$279

The MS6305-00 is a 10-inch dual-bevel sliding compound saw at a price where sliders usually get single-bevel or drop the slide entirely. Tech Gear Lab and Pro Tool Reviews both call it a strong value; PTR reports the 15-amp motor pushing through 2x material without bogging, and notes it cuts 2x12 and even 4x4 at 90 degrees (reported). Nine detents on a stainless miter plate cover the common angles, and the whole saw weighs a manageable 48.8 pounds.

Bevel range is the real limit here — 0 to 45 degrees each direction rather than the 47 to 49 of pricier saws — and the fit and finish sit below the DeWalt and Bosch. But for a DIYer building a first miter station, or a second saw for a remote corner of the shop, it delivers dual-bevel sliding capacity for roughly half the price of the name-brand 12-inch machines.

What to Know Before You Buy

// Sliding vs Non-Sliding: The Capacity Math

A non-sliding miter saw only cuts as wide as its blade will reach in a single chop. A 10-inch chop saw crosscuts roughly a 2x6 flat; a 12-inch chop saw gets you to about a 2x8. That is the whole ceiling — the blade comes straight down and back up.

A slider adds rails so the head travels forward through the cut, and width now depends on rail travel rather than blade diameter. That is how a 10-inch sliding saw like the Makita crosscuts a 2x12 or a 4x4 in one pass, and why 12-inch sliders such as the DWS780 reach 2x14. If you cut wide trim, shelving, or dimensional lumber wider than a 2x6, you need the slide. If you only ever cut 2x4s and narrow molding, a fixed chop saw is lighter, cheaper, and one less mechanism to keep square.

// Rails, Glide Arms, and Wall Clearance

The catch with a conventional slider is the rails: as the head slides forward to start a cut, the rails slide backward out the rear of the saw. That means a rail saw needs open space behind it — often 24 inches or more — or it cannot reach full capacity. In a shop where the bench sits against a wall, that space does not exist.

Two designs work around it. Bosch's axial-glide arm folds in place instead of sliding rails rearward, so the GCM12SD is rated to work with about a foot of clearance. Makita mounts the rails alongside the head rather than behind it, giving the LS1019L a similarly small rear footprint. Both cost more than a plain rail slider of equal capacity — you are paying for the geometry that lets the saw live against a wall. If your saw sits on an open stand in the middle of a garage, you do not need to pay for it.

Two more practical notes. First, dust: even the best miter saws throw a plume of fine dust, and a shop-vac hookup plus a little air filtration goes a long way toward keeping a garage breathable — see our guide to the best air purifiers. Second, power: cordless saws such as the DCS781 free you from the outlet, but for corded saws in a shed or detached garage without a circuit, a large portable power station can run a 15-amp saw for short sessions. And for cleanup between cuts, a cordless stick vacuum handles the sawdust that skips the dust port.

Bottom Line

For most buyers building a fixed station, the DeWalt DWS780 remains the saw the reviews keep pointing to: full 12-inch capacity, accurate out of the box, and priced reasonably on promotion. If your bench lives against a wall, the Bosch GCM12SD or the compact Makita LS1019L buy back the clearance a rail saw wastes. If you work away from power, the DeWalt DCS781 delivers 12-inch cuts off a single battery. And if the budget is the constraint, the Skil MS6305-00 puts dual-bevel sliding capacity within reach for under $300.
