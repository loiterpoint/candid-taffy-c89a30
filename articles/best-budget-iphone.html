How We Evaluate Budget iPhones

Let's be direct about the method, because it determines how much weight you should give the rest of this page. Loiter Point does not run a lab. We do not own test rigs, we do not run battery drain cycles, and we do not photograph color charts. Anyone who tells you they benchmarked five phones for a roundup like this in a week is usually recycling someone else's numbers without saying so.

What we do instead is synthesize. Every performance figure on this page comes from one of three places, and each is named where it appears:

  • Published independent lab measurements — primarily GSMArena, whose display and battery testing is repeatable and methodologically documented, and Consumer Reports, which runs its own battery lifespan protocol.
  • Apple's own published specifications — chip, display resolution, camera sensors, charging wattage, battery capacity, and Apple's own battery ratings. Apple's ratings are marketing figures produced under Apple's conditions; we label them as such rather than presenting them as measurements.
  • Dated price reporting — retail and refurbished pricing moves weekly, so every price here carries the date it was observed. Where sources disagree, we say so instead of picking the tidier number.

The ranking below is ordered by what the evidence supports for a buyer spending as little as possible, not by price and not by what pays best. That is why the cheapest phone on the page finishes fourth.

The Picks

#1 Best Overall

iPhone 17e

$599 (256GB)

The only current-generation new iPhone at budget money.

Apple launched the 17e on March 2, 2026 as its cheapest new iPhone: $599 for 256GB, $799 for 512GB, which puts it $200 below the entry iPhone 17. That positioning is the whole argument. If you want a brand-new, current-generation iPhone with a full warranty and the longest update runway available, this is the only door.

The measured numbers back it up. GSMArena's lab recorded brightness just over 800 nits on both a 75% white surface and a 10% white patch — notably, essentially the same figure on both, meaning the panel does not collapse when most of the screen is lit. On battery, GSMArena's lab returned an Active Use Score of 15:30h and 4K video playback of 16h18m. For context from the same lab: the iPhone 17 managed 19h10m and the much slimmer iPhone Air only 13h29m on that video test. Consumer Reports separately reports an above-average battery lifespan of 37 hours.

Elsewhere you get the A19 chip, Apple's new C1X cellular modem, a 48MP Fusion rear camera with an optical-quality 2x telephoto, Ceramic Shield 2 on the front rated for 3x better scratch resistance than the previous generation, and 15W MagSafe wireless charging — double the 7.5W the 16e allowed, and with actual magnetic alignment.

The compromise, stated plainly: the 6.1" Super Retina XDR OLED runs at 2532x1170 and 60Hz, not 120Hz. This is a real limitation, not a rounding error, and it is the single most visible way Apple separates the "e" line from the rest of the lineup. Nerd Box: Why 60Hz vs 120Hz Actually Matters

Both "e" phones — the 17e and the 16e — ship 60Hz OLED panels. Every other current iPhone family runs higher. A 60Hz display redraws the image sixty times a second, so a scrolling list moves in ~16.7ms steps; on a 120Hz panel those steps are half as long and half as far apart. Nothing is broken at 60Hz. It is simply less smooth in the two places your eye is best at catching it: fast scrolling through text, and dragging an object under your finger, where the on-screen element visibly lags the fingertip.

The honest framing is that this is a noticing problem, not a function problem. If you're coming from another 60Hz phone, you will likely never think about it. If you're coming down from a 120Hz iPhone or Android flagship, you will notice within an hour and you may keep noticing for weeks. There's also a second-order effect worth knowing: high-refresh panels on other iPhones can also drop down to save power and drive the always-on display. A fixed 60Hz panel can't do that variable trick — which is part of why the always-on feature isn't part of the "e" package.

The counterweight is what Apple spent the savings on. GSMArena's lab measured the 17e at just over 800 nits on both a 75% white surface and a 10% white patch, and that consistency across a bright full-screen load matters more for daily outdoor legibility than refresh rate does. Sunlight readability is a spec you fight with every day; refresh rate is one you stop seeing.

Nerd Box: What the C1X Modem Changes

The 17e ships Apple's new C1X cellular modem — the piece of silicon that talks to cell towers. Historically that part came from a third-party supplier; Apple bringing it in-house is one of the quieter but more consequential changes in the budget line, because the modem is one of the largest power draws in a phone that's hunting for signal.

We're not going to tell you what that translates to in bars of signal or megabits, because we don't have an independent measurement of the C1X in isolation and we aren't going to invent one. What we can point at is the battery result the whole package produces: GSMArena's lab logged an Active Use Score of 15:30h and 16h18m of 4K video playback, and Consumer Reports reports an above-average battery lifespan of 37 hours. Whatever the modem is contributing, the phone it's in tests well. If you live somewhere with genuinely marginal coverage, wait for carrier-specific field reporting before treating the modem swap as an upgrade or a risk.

Bottom Line

If you want a new phone and you want to stop thinking about it, buy the iPhone 17e at $599. It's the only current-generation iPhone at budget money, GSMArena's lab numbers for its screen and battery are strong, Consumer Reports backs up the battery story independently, and the 60Hz display is the one thing you're really giving up. That is a clean trade.

If you want the most phone per dollar and you're comfortable buying refurbished, the iPhone 16e is the smarter spend. Apple Certified Refurbished from $419 as of MacRumors' July 6, 2026 reporting, with guaranteed iOS updates through at least 2029, means you're giving up MagSafe, an ultrawide camera and mmWave — not longevity. That's the compromise most people should take.

The iPhone 16 only makes sense if the Dynamic Island specifically matters to you and it actually rings up at $699 rather than $799. Check the price. And the refurbished iPhone 13 is the right answer only when the budget is genuinely hard-capped near $200, because its short remaining support runway is a cost you pay later rather than at checkout.

One last piece of advice that costs nothing: for the two refurbished picks, buy from a seller with a real return window and a stated battery-health disclosure. Refurbished pricing here moves week to week, and a $30 saving on an unverified unit is not a saving.
