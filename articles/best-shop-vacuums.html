How We Evaluate

Loiter Point does not operate a suction rig or a dust chamber. What we do is read the outlets that run their own bench tests — Pro Tool Reviews, Bob Vila, Reviewed, TechGearLab — cross-check their findings against a large pool of owner reviews, and pull out the models that keep landing on lists for a defensible reason. When a number comes from a manufacturer's spec sheet, we tag it (rated). When it comes from someone who measured or used the vac, we tag it (reported). We do not blend the two.

Roles matter more than a single winner. A 16-gallon drum on casters is the wrong tool in a hall closet, and a 5-gallon wall unit is the wrong tool for a drywall gut job. So the five picks below are split by job: overall pick for most garages, a large heavy-duty drum, a value mid-size, a compact wall-mount, and a cordless jobsite vac.

On price: street prices in this category move week to week and swing hard around holiday tool sales. The figures here are approximate street prices observed in mid-July 2026 and are there for sizing the decision, not quoting a receipt. Check the retailer before you buy. Corded shop vacs in particular are frequently discounted below their list price.

01 — Best Overall
RIDGID RT1200 NXT 12-GallonHD EXCLUSIVE

The 12-gallon NXT is the model that shows up most often as a "buy this if you're not sure" pick, and the reasoning is consistent across outlets: a mid-size drum that is big enough for a garage but still fits in a car trunk, high rated airflow, and RIDGID's lifetime service agreement on the motor once you register it. One important buying note: RIDGID is sold through Home Depot in the US and is effectively a Home Depot house brand, so you will not find it at Lowe's. That is a distribution fact, not a knock.

When the mess is drywall dust, a garage full of leaves, or a remodel's worth of debris, capacity and hose diameter stop being spec-sheet trivia and start deciding whether you empty the drum twice or six times. 03 — Best Value
DeWalt DXV10P 10-GallonQUIET
~$110–130

The DXV10P is the pick for buyers who want a recognizable brand and a two-stage motor without paying pro-dust-extractor money. Its selling point in reviews is noise: the two-stage design runs noticeably quieter than a typical single-stage drum (reported), which matters more than people expect for a tool that otherwise makes you want to leave the room. It ships with a stainless-friendly poly tank, a full attachment kit, swiveling casters, and a built-in drain.

DeWalt's own listings label it anywhere from 5.0 to 5.5 peak HP depending on the revision and 90 CFM (rated) — another reminder that peak HP is a moving marketing target even within one product line. Owner reviews are broadly positive but not uniform: some report inconsistent suction and gripe about the hose-to-tank fit, so it is a value pick rather than a heavy-duty one. For a homeowner who cleans the garage and the car and occasionally sucks up water, it is enough vac and it is quiet.

What to Know Before You Buy

// Nerd Box — Why "Peak HP" is a Marketing Number

"Peak horsepower" is measured at the instant the motor stalls, under lab conditions the vac never sees in use, and often with the airflow blocked. It is not the power the vac delivers while cleaning, and it is not regulated the way an appliance's running watts are. That is why a 6.5 peak HP drum and a 5.0 peak HP drum can perform almost identically — and why a cordless brushless motor with no HP rating at all can out-suck both.

The two numbers that predict real pickup are airflow (CFM) — how much air moves through the hose, which carries debris — and sealed suction / water lift (inches of water), which is how hard the vac pulls when the nozzle is choked against a surface. Fine dust and dense material need suction; leaves and sawdust need airflow. When two vacs list the same peak HP but different CFM or water lift, believe the CFM and water lift. Manufacturers rarely publish both cleanly, which is itself telling.

// Nerd Box — Wet Pickup and the Filter Rule

Every vac here is a wet/dry vac, but "wet/dry" does not mean "leave the filter in for both." The standard rule from the makers: for large water pickup, remove the paper or pleated dust filter and use the foam sleeve (or the bare cage on some models). A soaked paper filter chokes airflow, can tear, and stays damp long enough to grow mildew. Run water through a dry-dust filter often enough and you will be buying replacements.

Two more habits that extend a shop vac's life: drain the tank through the built-in drain rather than tipping a full drum of water over the motor housing, and let the foam filter dry fully before switching back to dry pickup. If you are pulling standing water out of a basement or a flooded space, the vac gets the water out — but drying the space afterward is a job for a dehumidifier, not the vac.

Two related guides if a shop vac is more tool than you need: for interior car detailing a smaller, gentler machine is usually the right call — see our best car vacuums roundup. And for everyday floor cleaning inside the house, a shop vac is overkill and loud; a cordless stick vacuum is the better daily driver.

Bottom Line

For most buyers, the RIDGID RT1200 NXT 12-gallon is the safe default: enough drum for a garage, high rated airflow, and a lifetime motor warranty, with the only catch being that you buy it at Home Depot. Step up to the Craftsman 16-gallon if you generate real debris and want the wider hose and blower port. The DeWalt DXV10P is the quiet value play, the Vacmaster VWM510 gets the vac off the floor for space-tight garages, and the Milwaukee M18 FUEL is the one worth its high price only if you are already on M18 batteries or routinely work away from outlets. None of these numbers came from a Loiter Point bench — they came from the outlets and owners cited below, which is the point.
