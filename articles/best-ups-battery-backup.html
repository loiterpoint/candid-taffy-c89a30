How We Evaluate UPS Units

Loiter Point does not run a testing lab. We do not own an oscilloscope, a load bank, or a rack of sacrificial power supplies, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. What we do is synthesize three things: published manufacturer specifications, independent reviews from outlets that do run hardware through instrumented testing, and verified owner reports from forums and vendor support communities where the same failure patterns show up again and again.

That means every performance number on this page is attributed. When we say the APC BE600M1 delivers 23 minutes at a 100W load, that figure comes from APC's own published listing — it is a vendor claim, and vendor runtime claims are typically generated under ideal lab conditions with a fresh battery. When we point to the CyberPower CP900AVR as the pick for a home network, that reflects Wirecutter's 2024 recommendation, which cited runtime, ease of use, and compact size. When we describe how active-PFC power supplies misbehave on stepped-sine-wave units, that comes from discussion threads on Tom's Hardware, Schneider Electric's own APC community forum, and SilentPCReview — reported failure modes, not something we watched happen.

Where the evidence is thin, we say so. UPS units are a category with surprisingly little rigorous independent testing relative to how many of them get sold, and a lot of what circulates online is affiliate filler with invented numbers. Battery runtime in particular varies enormously with load, battery age, and ambient temperature, so treat any single runtime figure — including the manufacturer's — as a ceiling rather than a promise.

Prices below are approximate street prices, and UPS pricing swings hard with retailer promos and battery commodity costs. The CyberPower CP1500PFCLCD was tracking around $240 on Amazon in early July 2026 per camelcamelcamel; the APC BR1500MS2 has recently ranged roughly $260–$340 depending on retailer, with Staples listing it at $339.99. Simulated sine wave vs. active PFC: the failure mode that eats cheap UPS purchases

This is the single most common way people buy the wrong UPS, and it is worth understanding before you spend anything.

A simulated (stepped) sine wave UPS does not reproduce the smooth AC waveform from the wall. It approximates it with a stepped square-ish waveform that includes a brief zero-output gap at the phase change. Most simple electronics tolerate this fine. Power supplies with active power factor correction — which is essentially every modern desktop PSU worth owning — often do not, because active-PFC circuitry watches the incoming waveform closely and reacts badly to that discontinuity at the moment of transfer to battery.

Reported failure modes, drawn from discussion on Tom's Hardware forums, Schneider Electric's APC community forum, and SilentPCReview:

  • The UPS trips its own overload protection and shuts down when the PFC circuit draws a sharp inrush at transfer.
  • The PSU shuts the PC off during the transfer — the exact outage the UPS was bought to prevent.
  • Transfer time is too slow for the PSU to ride through on its hold-up capacitance, causing a reboot.

These are patterns reported by owners and vendor-forum participants, not behavior Loiter Point has observed. But they recur consistently enough, across independent venues, that we treat them as a real design constraint rather than folklore.

Practical rule: if your power supply is labeled "active PFC", buy a pure sine wave UPS. In this roundup that means the CP1500PFCLCD or the BR1500MS2. The CP900AVR, AVRG900LCD and BE600M1 are stepped-wave units and belong on networking gear, not on a desktop.

How to Size a UPS (and What It Is Not For)

Sizing is arithmetic, not intuition. Add up the continuous draw of everything you intend to plug in, then buy a UPS with roughly 20–30% headroom above that total. This is a general rule of thumb rather than a specification — it exists because batteries lose capacity as they age and because you will inevitably plug in one more thing.

As rough anchors: a typical desktop plus one monitor commonly lands somewhere in the 300–400W range, and gaming systems can exceed 600W. Those are general ranges, not measurements of any specific machine — check your own hardware's actual draw if you can. And watch the units: a UPS advertised as "900VA" may be rated at 560W or at 480W, as the CP900AVR and AVRG900LCD demonstrate. The watt rating is the one that constrains you. VA is the number marketing prefers.

Two expectations worth resetting. First, a UPS is for riding out brief outages and giving you time to shut down cleanly — it is not a generator, and it will not run your equipment for hours. Second, UPS batteries are consumables. They typically need replacing every few years, which is why a user-replaceable battery (as on the APC BR1500MS2) is a genuine long-term cost advantage over a sealed unit you have to discard.

Bottom Line

For most households and small offices, buy the CyberPower CP1500PFCLCD. At around $240 it delivers 1000W of rated capacity, pure sine wave output that sidesteps the active-PFC problem entirely, and 12 outlets — the best combination of headroom and flexibility in this group. It is the pick that will not need replacing when you add a second monitor or swap in a bigger GPU.

Choose the APC BR1500MS2 instead if PowerChute matters to you or if the user-replaceable battery is worth the premium over its service life — accepting that you are paying more for 100W less and two fewer outlets. If the load is purely networking gear, the CyberPower CP900AVR has the strongest independent endorsement here and costs a fraction as much. The AVRG900LCD is the answer when you need outlet count more than watts, and the APC BE600M1 is the cheapest defensible purchase in the category for a modem-and-router load.

Whatever you pick: check the waveform against your power supply, size on watts rather than VA, and verify the current price before you commit. Prices in this category move constantly, and the numbers above are snapshots, not guarantees.
