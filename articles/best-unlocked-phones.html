The Best Unlocked Phones

Buying unlocked is a different decision from buying a phone. You are trading a subsidised monthly line item for band compatibility homework, a manufacturer-direct warranty, and a device you actually own on day one. These are the five worth owning outright.

  • Best value, full stop: the Google Pixel 10a — $499 list, street lows of $449, and the same 7-year update promise. Best evidence-to-price ratio on this page.
  • Battery champion: the OnePlus 15 — a 7,300mAh cell and independently published endurance figures no one else here comes close to. Also ships dual-SIM, which matters if you travel.
  • If you need iOS: the Apple iPhone 17 at $799 with 256GB base storage — reviewers call it the value pick of the iPhone 17 family.

How We Evaluate Unlocked Phones

Loiter Point does not run a lab. We do not own test rigs, we do not have a rack of loaner units, and we are not going to pretend otherwise. What we do is synthesise published independent measurements from outlets that do run controlled tests — GSMArena, Tom's Guide, Notebookcheck, Android Central, Trusted Reviews, Droid-Life — and cross-check them against verified owner reports from long-term users. Every real-world number on this page is attributed to whoever actually produced it.

For the unlocked category specifically, we weight four things that a carrier-locked buying guide can afford to ignore:

  • Software support length, because it sets the resale floor. An unlocked phone is an asset you can sell; a phone that stops getting security patches in two years is not.
  • Band and VoLTE breadth on the US-market unlocked SKU — not the international variant, which is frequently a different radio configuration.
  • Battery endurance from named test methodologies, kept separate rather than blended, because different labs run different workloads.
  • Dual SIM / eSIM flexibility, which is most of the practical payoff of going unlocked in the first place.

Where published results disagree — and on the Pixel 10 Pro's battery they disagree sharply — we say so rather than picking the number that makes a cleaner sentence. Rows in the spec grids are labelled rated (manufacturer claim), reported (a named outlet's or owner's figure), or est. real-world. Nothing here is labelled "measured" unless a named lab measured it.

What Buying Unlocked Actually Gets You

The pitch is usually framed as "freedom," which is vague enough to be useless. Here is the concrete list.

No 24–36 month device-payment lock-in. A carrier's "free phone" is a multi-year instalment agreement with a service commitment stapled to it. Leave early and the remaining balance accelerates. Buying unlocked converts that into a one-time cost, and it decouples your handset from your plan — you can drop to a cheap MVNO the week after purchase without unwinding anything.

Carrier compatibility is now your homework. This is the real trade. Before you buy, check the specific model number against your carrier's supported-device list, and confirm VoLTE and Wi-Fi Calling provisioning — US carriers have retired their legacy voice networks, so a handset that isn't whitelisted for VoLTE on your network may show full bars and still not complete a call. Band support on paper is necessary but not sufficient; provisioning is the part people get burned on.

US "unlocked" is not the same as "international." Manufacturers routinely ship a North American unlocked SKU with a different band stack, and sometimes different modem or charging behaviour, than the global variant sold through grey-market importers. A listing that says "unlocked" and ships from overseas may be a genuinely different phone. Buy the US model, from a seller who says so plainly.

No carrier bloatware, and faster updates. Unlocked units generally ship clean and take manufacturer updates directly instead of waiting on carrier certification. On phones with long support windows, that gap compounds over years.

Warranty is manufacturer-direct. You deal with Google, Samsung, Apple or OnePlus rather than a retail store acting as an intermediary. In practice that usually means faster escalation and no argument about who owns the relationship — though it also means no walking into a carrier store for a same-day swap.

Dual SIM and eSIM actually get used. Two lines on one handset — work and personal, or domestic and a local travel eSIM — is the feature carrier-locked buyers most often can't fully exploit.

Resale value. An unlocked, paid-off, still-supported phone sells to anyone on any network. A locked one sells to a much smaller pool at a discount. Software support length is the biggest single input here, which is why the two Pixels rank where they do.

Nerd box: why you can't compare battery numbers across labs

You will see the OnePlus 15's GSMArena Active Use Score of 23:07 hours and Tom's Guide's 25 hours 13 minutes sitting next to each other and assume one of them is wrong. Neither is. They are answering different questions.

GSMArena's Active Use Score is a composite — it runs separate call, web, video and gaming loops (39:12, 21:48, 32:57 and 13:05 respectively on this phone) and folds them into a single synthetic figure representing a modelled usage mix. Tom's Guide runs its own continuous workload at a fixed display brightness. Different screen brightness targets, different network conditions, different proportion of GPU load, different idle behaviour. The scores are internally consistent within each outlet and meaningless across them.

The practical rule: only compare battery figures produced by the same lab under the same methodology. Tom's Guide's Pixel 10 Pro result (under 13h 45m) versus its OnePlus 15 result (25h 13m) is a fair comparison, because it's one test. Putting GSMArena's OnePlus number against Droid-Life's 7–8 hour screen-on-time observation for the S26 Ultra is not — screen-on time and active-use scores aren't even the same unit of measurement. This is the single most common way phone battery claims get laundered into nonsense, and it's why the spec grids above keep each source's number attached to its source.

Nerd box: silicon-nanostack, or how you fit 7,300mAh in a normal phone

Conventional lithium-ion cells use a graphite anode. Silicon holds substantially more lithium per unit volume than graphite does, but it swells as it charges, which historically wrecked cycle life. Silicon-nanostack designs — like the one in the OnePlus 15 — structure the silicon content so that expansion is accommodated rather than resisted, which is what allows a manufacturer to raise energy density without proportionally raising thickness.

That's the mechanism behind a 7,300mAh cell in a phone that doesn't feel like a brick, and it's why the endurance gap between the OnePlus 15 and the 4,870mAh Pixel 10 Pro is a chemistry-and-packaging story rather than a software-efficiency one. It also explains why battery capacity has jumped sharply in one generation after years of stagnation.

Bottom Line

Unlocked buying rewards people who think in years rather than months. The monthly-payment framing makes a $1,599 phone and a $499 phone look like the same decision separated by twenty dollars; buying outright makes the actual gap visible, which is exactly why carriers prefer the other model.

Given that, the Pixel 10a is the pick we'd hand to most people — 7 years of updates, the best camera evidence in its price class, and 30-plus hour endurance reported by Android Central, for $499 or less. It is the strongest cost-per-supported-year on this page and it protects your resale position better than phones costing three times as much. If you want the ceiling raised on photography and don't mind managing your screen-on time, the Pixel 10 Pro is the better camera and the same support window.

Heavy users should stop reading and buy the OnePlus 15 — the endurance advantage is documented by two independent labs running different workloads, and dual SIM makes it the traveller's default. iOS buyers want the iPhone 17 at $799 with 256GB. And the Galaxy S26 Ultra is genuinely excellent hardware that we'd only buy near its ~$1,079 street low, because the generational gains are moderate and $1,599.99 is a lot to pay for moderate.

Whichever you choose: verify the model number against your carrier's supported-device list, confirm VoLTE provisioning, and make sure you're buying the US unlocked SKU rather than an imported international variant with a different band stack. That five-minute check is the entire risk of buying unlocked, and it's avoidable.
